Your EM theories are not the problem.  Check the mechanical forces

[not quite - that's why a voltage regulator is part of the set-up.  When
electrical load demand is low, the regulator reduces field strength in the
alternator so that the alternator produces less electricity and thus requires
less mechanical rotational energy input(torque) - so although it is true that
the alternator rpm is mechanically tied to engine rpm, alternator torque
demand is a function of electrical load demand.  What changes in the output of
an alternator as a function of it's rpm is the pre-rectified alternating
current FREQUENCY, faster rpm, higher frequency and vice versa.  Since the AC
output is rectivied by 6 internal diodes to yield direct current and the
ripple in this rectified output is smoothed by the battery, the frequency of
the alternator's pre-rectified output  is not of concern (technically there
are some issues of concern, but they are handled via the integral design of
the alternator itself -has to do with inductance and hystersis ). - Harvey]
