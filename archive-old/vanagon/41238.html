Well, those brazilian (Brosol, I assume?) fuel pumps were never known
for high quality, but it seems unlikely that he picked up two bad ones
in a row. Time to stretch into the realm of less-likely-but-still-possible
guesses:

1.) Is his fuel pump pushrod the right length? They must be matched to the
pump. Don't know how many different lengths there were, except that there
were more than one. I think all 1600SP's came with the "tall" fuel pump.
But most Brosols I've seen, especially when sold by FLAPSs who were more
used to selling fuzzy mirror dice, were the shorties suitable for alternator
engines ('74-later bugs). If your pushrod is too long, it might overstress
the diaphragm inside the pump, and cause eventual (or immediate) holes in
the diaphragm.

2.) Does your friend make that 1/4 mile trip to work, twice a day, every
day? Plus longer (1/2 mile) trips to the post office, store, VW dealer?
He may be doing most of his driving with a cold engine and closed choke
plate. This causes rich mixtures (duh), fuel condensing on cylinder walls,
and maybe draining down into the crankcase. Normally even this isn't much,
but throw in worn rings (if any), misadjusted carb float level (if any),
and you could conceivably have a smallish river flowing from the carb,
down the intake manifold, into the cylinders, past the rings, and into
the case. It's a stretch, I know, but... Murphy's Law applies. My '71 bus
used to belch out huge clouds of acrid, opaque white smoke at odd times,
and I found the most amazing chain of weird coincidences combining to make
it do that. If it's crazy enough, someday somewhere a VW will do it,
whatever it is.

My $.02...
