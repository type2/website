Hi Kevin,
When VW went to the spade connectors the exterior square post that once was a 
screw connector was machined shorter.  Both the screw and spade terminal 
switches where this squre post goes through the plastic (Bakelite?) body of 
the switch housing.  This square post is machined to a smaller square 
diamensions and on the far rear end had a thin tube machined that later would 
be pressed into a hollow rivet.   To fabricate a male spade connector for 
easy manufacturing purposes they produced these out of brass flat stock with 
a square hole through them.  To install the male spade connectors they just 
slid these on the square post and inserted the square post throught the 
switch body.  The inside spring connector that rides/slides on the switching 
shaft/block inside was installed over the inside/rear end of each post and 
then the tube on the rear of the square shaft was pressed to swedge all these 
parts together.
  So in other words the spade terminal not only has the riveted connection to 
the spring connector that rides/slides on the switching shaft/block inside to 
cause voltage drop, but also where the flat brass spade connectors are pinned 
to the switch body by the forward end of the square brass shafts.  Resulting 
in not only one, but two places where corrosion can take place and cause both 
heat and voltage drop to dim/yellow your head lights! 
Best to put on some flux and solder on where the spade connector and square 
post meet nearest to the center body of the switch as possible.  Once you 
have the solder in this area apply the soldering iron to the farthest outside 
from the square post away from the center line of the switch.  This attracts 
the solder you have just put on the other inside of each square post to be 
sucked/drawn under the lip of the post securely soldering the spade clip to 
it!  Solder has a strange habit to actually follow the heat source that is 
melting it.

including how many passengers and where seated.  Then go out to darker
