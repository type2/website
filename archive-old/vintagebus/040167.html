I curious as to where you came up with 5400 lbs.  The placard in my 67 shows
a GWVR of something like 2000 kg, or roughly 4400 lbs.  My Chilton (I assume
for the early bay windows, Chilton is horrible at actually matching specs to
model years) lists the unladen weight of a kombi as 2624 lbs, with a 2171 lb
possible payload, adding up to a gross weight of 4795.  If you really have
loaded your bus down to the point that is weights 5400 lbs, 35/45 psi don't
seem at all out of line, though you would have to know where to weight is to
get the front/rear bias correct.  I'll bet you bus doesn't actually weight
that much, and you will wear the center of the tread at that pressure
though.  As an unscientific test: drive briefly and slowly though some dirt
(the should of a highway maybe), then get out and look at your tires.  Are
they dirty all the way to the edge of the tread?  If not, your pressures are
too high, and it will affect tire wear.  (Seeing this on my bus is what
convinced me that 40 psi was to much for the rear of my lightly loaded bus.)

This is true to an extent, car makers do often reccomend lower pressures
than those that generate best handling.  This is also their defense for
their tires blowing up on certain Ford vehicles, so anything BFS says on the
matter must be taken in that context as well though.

>the last thing he stated was that we should all go buy a GOOD tread
>gauge - 'properly inflated at ANY load will mean that the tread gauge
>shows ALL the channels to be the same depth straight across the top of
>the tire, ie, in a plane. if you are overinflated for your load, you
>will see a bulge with the gauge that is not visible to the eye; too  low
>will result in the side channels getting deeper.'
>

Actually, underinflation will wear the edges of the tire first, meaning less
tread depth on the edge, and more in the center.  But pressures are not the
only thing that affect uneven wear; wheel alignment, driving styles and
placement on the car also have an effect.  Front tires will tend to wear
more on the edges, and rear tires more in the center (on most rear-drive
cars at least).  Camber can make a big difference, so on at stock splitty
with something like +3 degrees of camber in the rear, i would not be
surprised to see the outside edge of the tire wearing faster than the inside
edge.

Also, fwiw, one does not need to buy a 'GOOD tread guage', a ruler and a
good eye work just fine.
