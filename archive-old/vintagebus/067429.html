>From: Claralw_at_aol_dot_com

>The hybrid car kind of uses this idea in that it sometimes runs on
>electricaity, and sometimes gasoline, and when running on gasoline
>it charges up the batteries.  This means lots les gas in used.  At
>least that's how I understand the hybrid cars.

That's basically true.  It uses the electric motor for low speeds
and acceleration, and the gas engine for higher speeds and steady
cruising.  The gas engine can be smaller than it would be without
the electric help, and can be tuned to operate over a narrower RPM
range, which is more efficient.  Another trick is "regenerative
braking" - you can recharge the battery when coasting or braking.
It's like compression braking, except instead of driving the pistons
mechanically against compression, you're driving a generator
electrically against the battery.

There is another kind of hybrid referred to as a "mild hybrid".  On
these, the starter and generator are combined into the same part and
are engaged with the powertrain all the time.  There is no other
electric traction motor and a relatively small battery pack.  The
engine can be shut down at a stop and the starter/generator is used
to restart it and move the car at low speeds.  They also do regenerative
braking.  Mild hybrids are usually implemented in conjunction with a
36/42 V electric system.

How to make biodiesel: Fill up the John Deere with diesel.  Go out and
plow the field and plant something.  After a while, fill up the JD again
and go spray fertilizer and pesticides (both petrochemicals) on it.
Finally, fill up the JD again and cut the stuff, dump it into a diesel
truck, and haul it to the processing plant.  There is some debate as to
whether you would be better off just putting the diesel in your car in
the first place - it is maybe not clear that biodiesel gives you more
energy than it costs to make it.

>The thing I don't get about this whole hybrid / elctric car thing is
>battery life is about 4-5 yrs.

For a pure electric car, this is true.  For a hybrid, the battery is
used less, and usually has a more intelligent controller regulating
its charge and discharge, so it lasts longer.  Toyota has a 10 year,
100k mile guarantee on the Prius battery pack.

>Once we end up with all of these old dead batteries laying around,
>what do we do with them?

You recycle them.  Lead-acid batteries are heavily recycled right now,
over 90% according to some studies.  There are pretty strict regulations
on their disposal, as well.  Other batteries, like NiCd and NiMH, can
be recycled as well.
