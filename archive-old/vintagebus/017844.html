I'd like to chime in on the ANSWER to Mark's problem..

Nope, not entirely correct (the part about the cell voltage and the common
"load test").  The cells will be 2.4v apiece under light operating conditions,
but if a complete (read: zero ohm or "big fat piece of well connected metal)
short is applied to a battery, it will get to damn near 0 volts -- because....
batteries have something called "internal resistance".  The battery itself
starts acting like a resistor.  Because of this, and assuming that the battery
has a complete (zero ohm) short on it, the cells will never reach 0v but will
come close.  Looking at the assumed shorted out starter, this is far from zero
ohms.  The "dynamic" resistance wil read differently from a static resistance,
i.e. measured cold, without power applied to it - with a standard ohmmeter
(which might in fact read zero ohms).  So the story gets complicated - a
battery that has variable potential (voltage) with the variation depending on
how much of a load you put on it, a shunt (in this case a shorted starter) of
a non-zero ohm value and alas the added variable of heat which changes this
shunt (heated wires gain resistance).

The point being that a strong battery can reach near 0v with a true zero ohm
shunt.  Probably won't reach too near that though, with a bad starter - and
shouldn't with a common load tester (it has resistance and a meter calibrated
to that resistance so that the readings will be of use to the mechanic.)  

But yes, the battery booster test should tell all about that battery oomph.
