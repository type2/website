Kyle wrote:

> Yup, turning also helps keep the supply chain moving. Why with a combination
> of metallic brake shoes carving away and frequent turnings, we can use
> enough brake drums to make sure they remain available.

exactly...
the brake parts people wouldn't mind, anyways... ;)
though i think most of the acvws' OE shoes and pads are organic, not metallic.
brake drums are not "forever parts"... they are going to eventually wear past
specified limits, whether or not you have them turned. The rule of thumb is to
replace any drum worn .060" over the standard size, if the discard number is
illegible or not present....
    organic vs. semi-metallic vs. full metallic... fade point ballparks: 550
degrees, 800 degrees, 1000 degrees, respectively... the metallics are harder on
drums and rotors, but the flip side to this is the higher fade points... more
brakes yet increased wear. still, IMHO, you can't go wrong with OE parts.... a
lot of engineering and metallurgy and different disciplines go into selecting
brake parts... the mantra: "o.e., o.e., o.e......"
    IIRC the last set of bus shoes i ordered was organic... if I had the option
of semi-metallics, I would give them a try... as I try to be easy on the brakes.
brakes will last a long time, given an attentive driver (as we all know....)..

> I still am amazed
> that accelerating the removal of material from the drums is considered to be
> a benefit. It defies logic.

turning the drums...
A. restores the drum's coefficient of friction. someone with worn-smooth drums
will not stop as quickly as someone who has had their drums turned...
B. trues the drum so that we have proper shoe-to-drum contact... helps for out
of roundess, taper...
    some claim that out of round drums will eventually return to normal... I
have heard of this on the List before... if the brakes get overly hot the drum
can become out of round... but this ought to be a signal, just like stepping on
a rake... that we could perhaps approach braking in a different light. i suppose
i shall have to start parking only in level spots... chock the wheels and sit
there till the drum temperatures go down... ;)
C. removes the build-up of "glaze" on the drum as well... ("glaze" is bonding
material that has melted out of the shoe lining, and collected on the
drum...)....
    making the vehicle stop as fast and as safely as it did when it left the
showroom is IMHO logical.

> I have been known to get in there and scratch
> them up with some good 40 grit sandpaper if they start squeaking, but I'll
> just let the material wear away as it is intended otherwise, thank you.

if you are a good sander-of-drums, you can pretty well knock out A and C of the
above... for the price of a good dial indicator/base/etc. to check B, though,
you could have A and B and C taken care of anyways... several times over.
