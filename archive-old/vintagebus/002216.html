The fork will be steel. It is used to move the sliding gear.
The synchro ring will always be a softer metal than the sliding gear and the
gear you are selecting. Go to Bentley, section H-7, page 8. Look at the pic
of the gear train. Unfortunately they don't show a fork. The synchro rings
are always loosely riding on the taper of each forward gear. [unlike reverse
which has no synchro and you are actually moving a true gear] The fork goes
into the groove of #10. Depending which gear you want, #10 moves left or
right, first contacting the ring #9 or #11. Let's say you are going from 3rd
from 4th. #10 is engaged with #8. The fork moves right and #10 disengages #8
+ #9. It then contacts #11 synchro ring. The ring always sits on the taper
section of #14, 4th gear. As shifting pressure is put on the ring, its job
is to 'synchronize' the speed of the sliding gear #10 to the speed of the
gear #14. #10 1st engages #11, #11 tightens on the taper of #14, #11 and #14
then move at the same speed, #10 then fully engages with #14 = 4th gear.
Ever have a car that grinds going into a gear. i.e. 3rd? And somebody tells
you ''the synchro is worn out''? Actually the synchro RING is worn out. 9
times out of 10, the inside taper of the ring is worn down and can no longer
slow down or speed up the gear it is riding on. The metal [bronze in VW's]
on the inside either wears away or is hardened and looses its friction
property [etched]. Then the speed of the sliding gear and the gear you want
is not 'synchronized' and it grinds.
Does any of this make sense?
