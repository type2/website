Look, you have a big fan pushing air out the botton of the bus.  If you
have a big opening nearby pulling air from the bottom of the bus into the
engine bay the air is going to take the path of least resistance, which
means that the air will be recirculated unless there is a big slipstream
to carry it away.  In addition, the airflow around the rear of a vehicle
isn't as smooth and predictable as we would like.  You may have gotten
away with this, but that doesn't mean the the people who follow your
advice will.

More about recirculation below.

Get out your ruler again.  At its _maximum_ extent, the "scoop"
extends _one_ inch from the body, and most of the scoop extends less than

within the boundary layer.  Since you're a "try it yourself" guy, I
suggest that you stick your finger one inch outside the window while
driving your bus and see how much slipstream you feel.

The shape of the inside of the ducts has nothing to do with the pressure
gain.  It's just a plenum, and its shape is determined by the exterior
contour of the bus.

I suggest that you check to see what work I _have_ done before making
such accusations.  This topic has been discussed previously although
the discussion centered upon missing engine bay seals, not discarding
the front tin.  I measured the difference in inlet and engine bay
temperatures and found the delta T to be about 3C under the
same conditions with good seals.  The important result was that missing
engine seals make a _big_ difference in engine bay temperatures, on the
order of 10-15C, due to recirculation.  Whether you acknowlege it or not,
recirculation can occur.
