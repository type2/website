Solomon Scott (Scott.Solomon_at_mcchord.af_dot_mil) wrote:
> 
> You are following good logic in your idea, but your method will not produce
> the kind of heat your after. Here is why. The stock blower from a 72-79 Bus
> was only to supplement the engine fan at low RPMs. If you hook one up and
> feel the output you will soon see that they put out very little. If you want
> real air flow into the cab, get a boat bilge blower. These are 12v and less
> than $25 ea. I have used these in the past with excellent results. Instead
> of mounting them at the pack and making them try to push hot air up the
> 12ft+ tube, mount 1 up front and make it pull the air from the back to the
> front and blow it out the vents. To do this you mount it under the front of
> the bus in line with the heat tube. You cut out a section that is just a
> little longer than the blower and slip it in. If you still want to make a
> recirc set-up, you can use the stock 72-79 blower out back to draw air from
> inside and then blow it down through the HEs then the front blower will do
> the rest. I speak here as one that has tried both ways. I have not found a
> blower that is strong enough to push air that far through a tube that size.
> You are better to draw the air though. There is info on this in the Library,
> and the archives. One suggestion I would make though is to use a headlight
> switch from a Dodge/Ford/GM. You want to run the power though the dimmer
> part of the switch, this way you can control the speed. I had one that was
> just on/off, it would cook you out in no time, but had no way to turn it
> down. It was also loud. The second Bus I did this to, I used a Dodge
> headlight switch, it allowed me to control the flow and noise level. I won't
> own a Bus now without one.

> Make sure that the blower has the little flaps on the outlets and instead of
> eliminating the stock setup, tee into it. That way, when you don't run the
> electric, the flaps will close and the engine fan will do its thing, whether
> the heater boxes are open or closed. When you do run the fan, it will open
> the flaps and push air, so long as the engine fan isn't pushing more
> pressure. The only time that it would be nice to have a second set of flaps
> on the engine side, is when the engine isn't running, because you could
> still use the fan for a few till the heat ran out. I suppose you would leak
> too much pressure into the fan shroud and wouldn't get any more heat. The
> bays had a relay that operated the fan only when the engine was idling, as
> soon as the alternator kicked up the relay shut off and the engine fan
> supplied all of the air.
