My understanding of valve float was that the weight of the valve itself
was too much for the spring to be able to pull it back in time, which
is why in the higher-revving engines, they use lighter valves which 
can respond quicker, and in *really* high-revving engines (F1, mracing bikes), 
they eliminate the valve & spring concept altogether (electic, desmodromic or 
pnuematic valves, among other technologies).  The dual spring setup 
helped the valve come back faster, at the potential detriment of cam
and valve seat wear.

Given the choice between misfiring and an engine teardown, I'll take
the misfiring.  There are rev limiters which do selective spark elimination,
but that's a slightly more complicated system than the rev-limiting rotor.
How about using a rev-limiting rotor, but one just below the grenade-point,
ie 7000RPM - _at_ that speed, you probably *do* want the engine to cut out
before it grenades.  On the 911, I was using the 7250rpm limiter, and
that was just above what that engine should have been doing, so if I hit it,
I'd get spanked and for good reason.

Once you get used to having a rev limiter, you learn to drive within it's 
bounds and you forget it's even there.
