The repair costs are higher but you don't have them as often.  Compared
to a new car, a Bus needs *lots* of maintenance, but it's a lot of very
simple things.  Newer cars need much less regular maintenance but when
something breaks, replacing it is either more involved, or you have to
replace a whole assembly instead of just the broken part of it.

For most of the computer-controlled cars from the 1980s through to 1995,
you don't even need a computer to get the trouble codes.  Usually you
could jumper a couple of pins on a connector and get the codes flashed
at you on the "check engine" light or read them with a $10 voltmeter.
With the advent of the OBD-II compliant computers in 1995-1996, some
manufacturers dropped this simple method of code retrieval and some
didn't.  A "real" professional OBD-II scan tool will run you about $500,
but you can get an interface that lets you use your existing PC for
about $100.  People complained when they had to buy tach-dwell meters
and strobe timing lights instead of filing points and tuning by ear;
now those two tools are cheap and standard.

Even with the computers, you still have to understand troubleshooting.
My mom's '89 Olds once lit the MIL and set a trouble code for the
camshaft position sensor.  I read the code with a paper clip, but
before changing out the sensor, I checked over the harness with an
ohm-meter to make sure it wasn't just a broken wire.  When one of
the ignition coils on the distributorless ignition on my '89 Chevy
went out, I got no MIL even after 40 miles of highway driving.  I
knew something was wrong because I had to floor it to do 60 and it
seemed to be using a lot of gas.  Pulling all the plugs revealed
two that weren't firing; tracing the plug wires showed these to
be on the same coil; rigging up a 12 volt test light to the coil
terminals proved that the module was not firing that coil.  When
the computer on that same Chevy failed after 100,000 miles, I got
no MIL and had to troubleshoot it using the factory procedure.  When
I was sure it was faulty, a rebuilt replacement was $100 exchange at
AutoZone, comparable to what a new 34-PICT-3 will run you.

I know I babble a lot about this but it's one of my pet subjects.
If you can read a manual and follow directions, there isn't much
difference between working on a 1951 Bus and a 2001 Eurovan, IMHO.
