> Aaron Borgman wrote:

> > Interesting.... it really shouldn't work right. My understanding of the
> > gas guage circuit is this:
> >
> > +12v-------------
> >                 |-
> >                 /-----
> >                 \    |
> >             R1  /    V
> >                 \    |
> >                 |-----
> >                 |+
> >                 |
> >                 /
> >                 \<----
> >             R2  /
> >                 \
> >                 |
> >                 |
> >                GND
> >
> >
> > Where R1 is the internal guage resistance, and R2 is the sender
> > resistance. The gas guage itself should just be a basic voltmeter. So
> > basically what the gas guage is measuring is voltage across it's internal
> > resistance. Therefore, the equation for this is:
> >
> > Vr1(voltage drop across r1) = Sv(supply voltage) * R1
> >                               -----------------------
> >                                 R1   +   R2
> >
> > The interesting thing is: R2 >>>>>>>>R1 most of the time.
> >
> > In this case, R2 dominates the equation to some extent. For example:
> >
> > Assume R1=1, R2=100(empty) -> 0(full), 6v source for a correct 6v system
> >
> > Vr1 = 6v * 1 ohm
> >       -----------       =   .06v        Full tank
> >         101 ohm
> >
> > Vr1 = 6v * 1 ohm
> >       ----------        =    6v         empty tank
> >           1 ohm
> >
> > If you change to 12v, but keep the 6v sender and guage:
> >
> > Vr1 = 12v * 1 ohm
> >       -----------       =    .12v       ~1/2 tank
> >         101 ohm
> >
> > Vr1 = 12v * 1 ohm       =    12v        really empty tank
> >       -----------
> >           1 ohm
> >
