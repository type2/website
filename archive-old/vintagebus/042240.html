> Stock springs just barely

The only function that valve springs have is to return the valves to a
closed position after they are opened.  They must be strong enough to keep
the  the lifter in contact with the cam while the valve is closing.  At
high RPMs, the spring has to be stronger as the valve must close faster,
which is why race engines run stronger valve springs.  VW designed the
stock valve springs to provide sufficient force to close the valves at
normal RPM levels.

> and HD singles aren't going to kill your drive train.

One disadvantage of using stronger valve springs is increased wear on the
valve train.  That extra force on the cam lobe will cause increased wear
and more energy will be used up in operating the valve train.  There is no
reason to increase pressure unless you are building a specialized race
engine.

I come from a philosophy of defining what it is you want to accomplish
before you start.  Running race parts without good reason can have
negative effects on reliability, as I explained above, and can be bad for
one's pocketbook.  The VW magazines provide plenty of hype in this
direction and I try to be a moderating voice.  You'll note that I'm not a
total stock nazi, since I happily run an alternator and late bay discs and
calipers.

The VW engineers worked very hard to make a 1600cc engine that could
produce full rated power continuously and reliably in a bus.  That engine
can be tweaked to produce more power for a short time, but getting more
power continuously is _very_ difficult with the stock cooling system.
When confronted with the need for increased power the VW engineers built
an entirely new type 4 engine for the task.  

You _can_ get away with a large bore type 1 engine in a light car (VW do
Brasil did exactly that with a 1700 type 1 in their sports car) but you
only get away with it because you do not use full power for extended
periods as you do in a bus.
