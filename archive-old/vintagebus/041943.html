Well, first of all, where would aluminum rub against the bearing?  The
flywheel is steel.

Second, bearings almost always are made of dissimilar metals, so using the
shims as thrust bearings wouldn't work too well.  I doubt that they move
much relative to each other in operation.

the shim-thrust bearing interface is designed to move, with lubrication
designed to keep the shim and bearing separated through constant supply of
lubricant from the bearing.

Third, in a very similar engine, Porsche uses a single shim.

My take on this is that VW decided to use multiple shims so that a stack
of any thickness could be made up from a limited variety of shims.  The
Porsche approach requires a greater variety of shims be stocked, but a
thicker shim is much less likely to be deformed by the stronger pressure
plate in Porsche vehicles.

Three is the number in VWs, but be sure to put the thinnest shim in the
middle!
