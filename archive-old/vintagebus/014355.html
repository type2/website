> Karl wrote:
> 
> > An even easier way to adjust valves:
> > Turn engine in direction of rotation. When exhaust
> valve JUST starts to
> > open, adjust intake. Rotate engine, watch exhaust
> open then close, watch
> > intake open then close. JUST as the intake closes,
> adjust the exhaust.
> > Go to next cylinder. Same procedure. Takes longer
> to R + R valve covers than
> > to adjust. Don't need to watch dist. rotor or
> timing mark.
> > 
> 
> Again, this method is perfectly acceptable IF and
> ONLY IF you are using
> a stock camshaft. ANY of the hot- medium- or mild-
> grind cams with a
> non-stock ramp to the lobes may very well be far
> from the lowest point
> on the lobe ( which is where you want the valve play
> set from) if you
> use this method, and you will end up with loose
> valves a clattering.
> Loose valves not only cut down power, but as Stephen
> pointed out
> earlier, a loose valve will not cool as well,
> because heat dissipation
> onto the seat surface of the head will be lessened. 
> If you are going to try one of these methods on an
> angine you yourself
> did not assemble, I would check all valve clearances
> the old-fashioned
> one cyclinder-at-a-time method after you do it, the
> very first time. If
> all is well, you most likely have a stock or close
> enough to stock cam
> that you can go with this method.
