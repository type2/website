Here are a couple of my earlier posts on this subject.  One is the
"official" way and another is the "how can I make this work with
stuff I can buy at AutoZone at 7pm on Sunday" way. 

Official way
---
Bentley specifies the single-speed wiper motor at 20 watts fully
loaded.  It also lists the system voltages of the various generators
at anywhere from 6.3 to 7.3V.

P=VI, 20=6.3I, I=3.2A .  V=IR, 6.3=3.3R, R=2.0 ohms.
P=VI, 20=7.3I, I=2.7A .  V=IR, 7.3=2.7R, R=2.6 ohms.

So, you'd need a resistor valued somewhere between 2.0 and 2.6 ohms
and rated 20 watts or better (25W and 50W types are commonly
available).   These resistors will generally be in a finned aluminum
housing that can be bolted down to a surface, or in a sand/ceramic
housing that mounts via its connecting leads.  The resistor WILL
get hot so be careful where you mount it.

4 of Radio Shack's #271-120 $1.49 (8 ohm, 20 watt) resistors in 
parallel might work.  Otherwise, order one from an electronics
supplier...prices in a couple of catalogs I have range from
$2.97/ea to $4.80/ea for 2.0 to 2.5 ohm, 25 and 50 watt resistors,
in the finned aluminum case.
---

Field repair
---
Per Bentley the 6v wiper motor draws 20 watts.  It occurs to me
that this is a decent match for both filaments of a 6v tail-light
bulb in parallel (1154, 16.8W + 5.3W = 22.1W) or just use the
bright filament to get a little more speed out of the motor
without hitting it with the full 12v.  What I'm trying to say is
that a tail light wired in-line in the wiper motor hot lead might
be a good field expedient replacement for a volt-a-drop.  The
bulb will light up and get warm, though, so you will want to
put it where it won't glare on the windshield or ignite anything.
