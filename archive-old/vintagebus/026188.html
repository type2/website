> The way to read the bolt specs is this:
>
> M Metric
> 12 Shaft ( not head) diameter in mm ( millimeters) 12 mm or 1.2 cm
>
> 1.5 Thread pitch 1.5 MM between threads ( in english this number is
> expressed in threads per inch, metric uses the actual distance between
> threads) In metric there are 5 or more thread pitches available for
> every diameter shaft. In english there are usually only 2, UNC and UNF
> (coarse and fine)
>
> 25 length of shaft from where shaft meets head ( not outside of head) in
> MM ( 2.5 cm)
>
> ( Is this right for a spring plate bolt?  I thought they were longer)
>
> DIN 961 is a metric/German standard that means that it is a hex head
> bolt, with the head size standard ratio in proportion to the shaft
> diameter size, keeping the mechanical advantage of the bolt in a
> relatively standard range...
>
> Now, the thing that you are not told by the  bolt number is the tensile
> strength, what we in "'merica" call "bolt grade" and what them metric
> types call "property class". The VW number(N 10 119 1), however, does
> have a property class specification. You should use a bolt with metric
> property class at least 10.9 ( stamped on the end head of the bolt)
> which is roughly equivalent to a SAE grade 8. You could use a 9.8
> property class bolt, and you would probably be ok, assuming it isnt the
> only bolt carrying the weight. This is equivalent to a SAE grade 7 bolt.
> Grade 7 bolt has a tensile strength rating of 133,000psi minimum, where
> a grade 8 has a 150000 strenght rating. So if you are as fat as me, use
> the stronger one....:)
