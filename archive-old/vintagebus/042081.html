Somebody suggested it, but I decided not to add one.  No need, as that

> The plastic battery boxes are essentially an oddly-proportioned dish pan,
> and the only way they can leak fluid is if they are holed/cracked, or filled
> to overflowing.  I've used them, both Grp. 24 and Grp. 27 sizes,  for over
> 25 years in boats, trucks, and VW buses and have never had any kind of
> failure despite some truly severe conditions.
> 
> A plastic battery box may not be "correct" in a VW bus, but it virtually
> guarantees that there won't be any acid damage to surfaces or components
> adjacent to the battery.

Very true.  I've been putting off adding one, but if I do it now I can
just replace the center part of the battery tray.  If I wait, the edges
will rust out and it'll be major repair time.

I just replaced the battery and had a heck of a time with the fine folks
at Pep Boys.  Normally they do the pro-rate thing with no-fuss,
particularly since I actually keep the receipts.  This time they gave me a
hard time since I habitually use a group 24 battery in the bus, not the
bug-size battery specified by their computer.  After a couple of tries the
counter guy said "I just can't figure out how to make it accept this."
"How about if I enter it as an '84 toyota?"  I didn't think much of this
idea, since next time they might demand to see the vehicle (never have
before, but who knows?)  Finally they got the computer-savvy guy there and
he did a "manual adjustment" and did the deed for me.

This may just be a fluke, but it makes me a bit leery of pep boys.  Still,
it's better than some parts houses that insist upon doing a "charging
system check" that does god-knows-what to the systems on my bus that I
already know are just fine.
