>I am not intersted in blinding people I just want them to glow a bit,

You could put the low-beam filaments of each bulb in series, putting 6 V
across each filament.  Or, you could put the high and low beam filaments
of one bulb in series, putting about 6 V across each filament.

Anyway.  The "official" way to run the headlights is on 12 V DC.  Radio
Shack does sell a beefy power supply that will do this: 22-506, 12 V 10 A,
$100.  If you run 6012 bulbs with both low beam filaments in series, their
22-504, 12 V 3 A, $40 will probably do the job as well.

You can also do it from a car battery charger.  You can use a car battery
with the charger, but doing that will also make hydrogen, which usually
isn't so great inside your house.  Using just the charger works fine,
as long as it's a relatively "dumb" charger without automatic shutoff.
"Smarter" chargers will refuse to turn on if there isn't a minimum
voltage present; this is to help prevent backwards hookups and sparks.
Usually, the cheaper chargers will work better in this application.
You'll need 12 V at 8 A if you want to run the lights at full power and
12 V 4 A for lights in series.  Some chargers have a 6 V / 12 V switch;
you could wire the lights in parallel and run them on 6 V to get the
"dim" effect.

If you don't mind hauling a battery around, you could put a battery on
the bar and then haul it out to the garage or porch to charge it with a
charger you already have when it gets run down.  A "deep cycle" battery
will work better than a normal starting battery in this application.

Another option would be to use an old or surplus power supply from a PC.
You could run the headlights from the 12 V output, but you might also have
to put a small load (tail light bulb) on the 5 V output to get it to work.
Or, you could just run the headlights on the 5 V output directly.

Since you don't _have_ to be compatible with a battery, you could also
run the headlights on AC directly from a transformer.  The ones they
sell to run outdoor lighting will work just fine.  You need about 100
W to run both headlights at full power, or about 50 W to run the low
beams in series.  If the transformer has a photocell on it, put a piece
of electrical tape over it.

I hope this helps!
