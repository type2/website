hm- to relieve some of the monotony, I decided to try an experiment and see
how hot the engine ran at different rpms.   This was in Chris' 65 which has a
big motor, and had a roof rack, and was still somewhat laden. The road was
flat.   We stayed at each speed long enough (at least minutes) to feel that
the engine temp had stabilized.   Temps of the engine oil will of course vary
from vehicle to vehicle, depending on what if anything is on the roof, hills,
or lack thereof, oil cooler type, etc.   Weather is a factor, not just
temperature, but humidity.   And other things.   I am saying this as a reason
why I hesitant to post these figures,as they do not reflect your vehicle in
specifics .   What I do believe to be applicable is the curve.   I talked
before about on the one hand, heat produced by the motor goes up with rpms.
And on the other hand cooling also goes up with rpms.  The gripping hand is
how these factors combine  

 SO...

What does that mean?   

as the rpms rise, heat produced and heat blown away does not happen at the
same rate.   


RPM        OIL TEMP
2200           210
2400           210
2600           205
2800           210
3000           215
3200           220
3400           225

I may have this slightly off, as I am reciting from memory but the gist is the
same.

Though amount of air being blown rises , at a certain point the heat produced
rises more.    It seemed that 2600 RPM was the optimum cooling engine speed.
The engine ran a bit hotter  at lesser rpms, and also ran hotter at higher
rpms.    Max torque is higher, about 2800 rpms.  

So, if you are runnning hot, don't back down to 2000 rpms, go to 2600.
