>Thom, 
>
>Don't even think about changing the CAPACITOR at the bottom of the motor.
> This device is for noise reduction to the rest of the electrical system.  By
>noise reduction I don't mean audible noise, more than I mean frequency.  The
>frequencies generated by the dc brush motors in the fan assembly cause
>"noise" that can mess up something as small as your gas gauge.  this is a .4
>MicroFarrad Capacitor.  And unless your motors don't run, but ohm out ok,
>don't mess with them...
>
>I have restored quite a few ambulance fans (which I do, along with providing
>other VW related services), and have always opted for the voltage drop method
>of installation.  It really is not that bad a thing to install this device
>into your system.  Just make sure that where ever you mount it, that there is
>plenty of circulation or it is fixed firmly to a body panel of some sort, for
>heat dissapation.  Those little buggers get pretty hot under load.  
>
>I suppose if you want to spend more than the fan is worth, you can take the
>motors down to a generator shop or motor manufacturer and have them wind them
>with new wire for 12 volt operation, but the voltage drop is really a safe
>and economic way to go.  
>
>By the way, don't use any sandpaper or other course media on the commutator
>when you clean it up.  Use only Brasso type liquid metal cleaner or a paste
>like semi-chrome polish.  And to ensure long life, take some Q-Tips and
>alocohol and clean the brush material from the recesses in the commutator.
> This buildup of carbon can cause shorts in the motor assemblies, arking of
>power, and reduce the life of the motor.
