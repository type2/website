Zach,

Excellent questions, hope you don't mind if I give my 2 cents? I have the
exact same setup on my '78. '77 solid lifter motor etc...

You are definitely on the right track with your heater box flanges. Mine
were pretty warped. I whacked the edges with a hand-sledge and went after
them with a flap disc on my grinder and air grinder. Finally followed by a
session with my belt sander and finished off with a fine hand-rasp. When I
got to the hand-rasp stage, I threw some sharpie down to make sure
everything was true.

I didn't get to correct the header flanges on the vise because I forgot to
do it when they were off. I just crawled under and did the same job with
them mounted. Used one gasket, a little orange high-temp sealant and nary a
leak they have, even after several high altitude, high-heat beatings.

Don't bother doubling up the gaskets, it just wastes a couple of gaskets.
Might as well do it right.

Don't sweat the tranny seeping. I have yet to see an OG tranny without some
sort of crust or wet. Just check the level every quarter (4 months). I have
it programmed into my computer's calendar to remind me of maintenance items.

The oil leak at the bellhousing is most likely an old seep. If there is
nothing fresh, it may have been a pre-rebuild buildup. Some mechanics like
to clean up an engine, some don't.

The oil at the front of the case needs to be run down. Throw a new Dizzy
seal (super quickie test). It won't change the timing if you don't loosen
the clamp. Check your dipstick tube, not a quick fix, but a simple one. They
can be a little tight to get on, just use some grease a small dull pry tool,
it will slip right on. (word to the wise, buy two since they are so cheap)

If you rule those out, re-post. BUT, if you can live with a dirty engine,
wait for the day you pull it to re-seal it. I do it to every bus I have
owned, but I'm a neat engine-bay freak.

Timing is a cinch, go for it! The Bentley has a great primer as does the
Type2 articles. You really can't hurt anything unless you are wearing a tie
and are missing your fan shroud.

The hot-spark can be installed without removing your dizzy. I would though.
Just don't move the clamp, keep the wires on the cap so you know they are
correct and yank it. Follow the instructions implicitly! They need silver
oxide thermal paste and a resistor block that is not included (Radio Shack
and Napa Auto Parts). Just email the hot-spark guys with questions. They
have the best customer service I have ever seen in the VW world (tied with
Wolfsburg West). They actually LIKE answering questions and really support
their product.

Dwell is pre-set on an electronic points replacement, so it isn't applicable
to the Hot-spark.

FYI: "Dwell angle, a term used in automotive context, indicating the degrees
of rotation of the distributor cam during which the ignition contact breaker
points in the distributor are closed." Basically where and when the lobes of
the Dizzy hit the points to open the points which then allow spark. If your
dwell is off, your spark can either be too short or too long. The timing is
when the spark ignites the fuel at different locations of the piston's
combustion stroke.(LISTEES PLEASE ADD CORRECTIONS IF IM OFF HERE)

Hope this helps, ain't buses fun? Woohoo!
