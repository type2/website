Let me begin by saying that nobody is questioning your expertise.  And I'm 
sure that your experiences are just as valid as mine.
In a recent post a gentleman stated that according to the owner of a large VW 
repair shop when the speed limit was lowered to 55 MPH the number of busses 
that came in for engine work dropped by a large fraction.  This has been my 
experience also.  
I'm not a professional mechanic.  But I have years of experience with an air 
cooled bus.  And unlike you I have had problems.  The first engine in my bus 
lasted only 65,000 miles.  When I rebuilt it both heads were badly cracked 
and it had a burnt valve.  I had been driving at 65 to 70 MPH.  The "common 
knowledge" was that '71 busses had more power and with the relocation on the 
oil cooler they didn't overheat.  A CHT gauge would have told me differently.
Then I built an engine and didn't set the compression ratio correctly.  That 
one ended up with a holed piston after 35,000 miles.  I had an oil temp gauge 
at the time but it had a poorly designed sending unit that only read the case 
temp and by the time the case got hot enough for me to notice it was too 
late.  A CHT gauge would have saved that engine.
The next engine I had built by a small shop in Modesto, CA.  I had a dipstick 
oil sender installed at that time and a CHT gauge.  I drove by my head temp 
keeping it at or below 350 while cruising and below 400 while climbing hills. 
Sometimes the temp would go up to 425 but only for a short period.  That 
engine was going strong at 138,000 miles.  The reason I rebuilt it is that 
the transaxle failed the engine had lots of miles on it and it was out of the 
car.

Overheating in an air cooled engine is very hard to define.  It's impossible 
to detect in a stock VW because there isn't a gauge.  To my way of thinking 
though if a bus driven at normal highway speeds 65 to 70 goes through engines

I'm impressed, your family collectively drives air cooled VW's over 71,000

Unlike you I don't go through cars very fast. I've owned the same bus for

The average person who owns a bus doesn't have a clue about this stuff.  They

gauge will tell them though.  And if they pay attention and do regular 
maintenance they can expect to double the life of their engine.
