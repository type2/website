> Andy and Listees,
>    You asked...."has anyone ever fixed a cracked brake fluid reservoir."
>
>    I've got to go against the list wisdom on this one.  When we got our '71
> the brake reservoir was cracked and leaking and I couldn't find one new or
> used.  I decided that I didn't have anything to loose by trying a repair as
> I wouldn't use the reservoir as it was anyway.
>    I used a low wattage(15 watts I think), pencil type soldering iron to
> melt the plastic of the reservoir.  First thing was to thoroughly clean the
> reservoir inside and out with the last step being a water rinse.  After the
> reservoir was completely dry I attempted the repair.
>   I used the soldering iron to melt the plastic at the crack using a
> circular motion, like when you weld.  Make sure the "melt" goes as deep as
> possible and isn't just on the surface.  This left a depression along the
> length of the crack.  Then I used the iron to melt and move plastic from the
> surrounding area to fill in the depression.  Be patient with this as it
> doesn't happen very fast.  Then I let the whole thing cool off.  All of this
> left a very uneven surface on the reservoir.  I used course sandpaper to
> smooth off the high spots so that I could add reinforcement to the outside
> of the reservoir.  As a reinforcement I cut a piece of fiberglass window
> screen the size and shape of that side of the reservoir.  Then I put a layer
> of a product known as proseal on the reservoir and embedded the reinforcing
> screen into it.   I put it aside for a couple of days, giving the proseal a
> chance to thoroughly set. Proseal is a product used in aviation, it's a
> sealant that,when dry, is flexible, and is resistant to a host of solvents
> and chemicals.  You might be able to find a good sealant in a marine store.
>    Anyway, I did that repair over 2 years ago and that reservoir hasn't
> leaked a drop since.  But yes, I do still check on it to make sure it's
> holding up.
