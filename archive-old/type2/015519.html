removing as much risk as possible. Cost includes time/money for unnecessary

because you can't do what you said you would do this weekend/month, calling
too many favors from former helpful friends or the time to pay back the

perfectly and most proper economic exchange of extremely high value, but it

Do, indeed, add value for the knowledge gained and skills developed with
the time. On the other side of the equation is cost to hire the work done.
If it takes you 4 hours to perform a correct procedure that would take a
skilled mechanic 1 hour to do and his rate is $70/hour, your hours were
worth $17.50. If you take home $20/hr in your vocation, hiring the work out
is ecomically feasable as a possible enhancement of the cost/mile ratio.
If you take home less, then consider the immediate expense of purchasing
the manuals/tools required to perform the procedure. Know that the next
time you perform that procedure, it will take you maybe 2 hours and they
will be valued at $35 each.

Buy quality tools with the money saved. If you do a procedure in a weekend
that would cost $500 to hire, buy the tools with the $500 and gain the
knowledge for future enhanced value in reuse. I do not advise Craftsman.
Their spans are typically cut just a bit too wide. Spec is tighter on the
better manufacturers. DIN standard wrenches are a bargain for spec work,
but for rusty nuts a few spezial items will be needed.
