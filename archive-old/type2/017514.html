Hello, all.

I've just got around to thinking about the 1600DP I pulled from my bus a few
years ago, and thought it would be kind of fun to start it up, and have a go at
converting it to a compressor (1&2 firing, 3&4 pumping) until the time comes to
rebuild it to go in the bus. I pulled the engine because of excessive crank end
float, and the fact that I couldn't get a crank oil seal to last more than a few
weeks. The flywheel had been swapped over as part of the engine swap, and the
flywheel on this engine was left loose, so I thought I'd have a go at setting
the crank end float.

The initial measurement (with the original shims) was 0.8mm (0.032")! Took the
flywheel off again, made sure everything was clean, and tried again. Still the
same. "Ok", I thought, "It's not the original fly-wheel, perhaps these things
happen". So I pulled the original shims (with a bent paperclip), Yep, all 3 are
there, and I stick in an additional couple of spare shims, and start to put
everything back together. As I tighten the gland nut, the engine starts to lock
up; "OK", I thought, "I've over done it with the shims. Strange, since I only
added 0.6mm(0.024"). Must be dirt in the works". So I start to slacken things,
and the flywheel turns about 5 degrees before coming to a dead (metal against
metal) stop! Out of curiosity, I nipped the gland nut up a bit more. The
flywheel still turns easily to and fro through about 5 degrees. Since I now
should have #1 main clamped to the crank, this must mean that it's turning in
the case, yes? Also, there is still crank end-float in this condition, much the
same as before. I can't detect any *radial* play in the end of the crank.

So the question is: Am I on the right lines with the diagnosis, and if I am, is
it worth keeping this engine with a view to rebuilding it myself, or are the
cases likely to be scrap because of the loose main bearing hammering around ?

What can line boring recover ?

Your collective wisdom would be appreciated.
