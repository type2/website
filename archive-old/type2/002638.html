> John,
>
> I am gonna take a shot at describing my experience with making a dip stick
> temp gauge. I think it works great and cost me about $25. I believe there
is
> a post in the library by Mike Benthin, which is where I got the idea. Mike
> was glad to help me along.
> You need:
> 1. A digital meat thermometer. There are several types at Wal-Mart. The
one
> I used cost $20. It is different than most in that the digital readout
> screen is remote from the metal probe. The probe (imagine a stainless
steel
> ball-point pen refill) has the sensor in its tip. two wires run from this
> sensor and out the top of the probe. The probe is crimped where the wires
> exit. The wires continue for about four feet and are molded into a plug
> which looks sorta like a plug for headsets. The plug can be plugged into
the
> digital read out box, which is about the size of a wallet. Now, the probe
is
> not straight. It curves at its top, forming a handle for pushing/pulling
the
> probe into/out of meats.  This needs to be straightened out, which I did
for
> the most part by hand. I used the little side table on my Westy as a soft
> vice to finish the job. Now, this straightening process may form some
crimps
> which will be of no consequence.
> 2. You need a piece of brass tubing, the OD of which will fit down you
> dip-stick tube. It has to have a ID that will accommodate the
> now-straightened probe. I found such a tube at only one place, a hobby
shop,
> and they had plenty of them in different sizes. Bring the probe and your
> dipstick with you when you go to buy the tube. This will ensure that you
buy
> a tube of the correct length and diameter. The crimps might make the probe
> slightly wider at points. Gently file these down if necessary.
> 3. You need some 2 part, slow-setting epoxy.
> 4. You need some wire which will be spliced into the 4 foot wire provided
> with the thermometer. I used doorbell wire.
> 5, You need a washer that will go around the brass tube and act as a dip
> stick stop.
> 6. You need a small piece of rubber hose that will go around the top of
the
> brass tube and act as a handle as well as keep the wires that will run up
> the tube from the now-set-in-epoxy probe from crimping.
> I will describe the rest as briefly as I can. Cut the provided wire near
the
> plug. Run the wires up through the tube. Epoxy probe into tube, making
sure
> it is set to extend approximately 3" beyond bottom of tube. Fill tube
> w/epoxy as best you can. Epoxy washer at correct height to allow tip of
> probe to stop its downward travel just sort of the bottom of the sump. Run
> wires through piece of rubber hose then push hose onto tube. Splice about
12
> feet of wire to wires coming out of probe. Run wires to front of bus then
> splice wires to plug.  Plug into digital thing. Voila! Now you can monitor
> you oil temp, you engine compartment temp, the ambient temp, and yes, even
> your body temp!  Now, this is important. I said there were two wires, but
> that is not exactly correct, although it did make it a little easier to
> describe this process. Actually there is one wire inside a metal/mesh
> sheath. The sheath itself acts as the second wire. The wire inside the
> sheath gets spliced.  A wire must also be secured to both ends of the
> sheath. I simply slid a piece of wire under each end of the cut sheath and
> secured with epoxy. There are different types of thermometers and
obviously
> different ways of making this thing. I had no soldering gun,  so
everything
> got glued together. The design of the meat thermometer with its remote,
> plug-in read screen out made it superior to those of uni-construction
which
> involve disassembly and soldering some rather short, delicate wires. One
> other point: I keep my regular dipstick in the engine compartment to take
> oil measurements as I could not get a good reading off the brass tube.
