The trick is to understand the 2 different numbers in the designation of =
a multi-grade oil.  The first number is the viscosity at ambient =
temperatures.  In the case of 10w40 the oil has the properties of a 10 =
weight oil at ambient.  The second number designates the qualities of =
the oil at 100 degrees C.  So, at 100 degrees C, the 10w40 oil has the =
same viscosity a 40 weight oil would at 100 degrees C.  It's not that =
the 10w40 thickens with temperature, it's that it doesn't thin as fast =
as a straight weight oil does.  That is accomplished by the additives in =
the oil.   So, the multi-grade oil gives us the best of both worlds.  We =
can have thin for starting and at operating temperature still have the =
lubrication the same as a heavier grade.  Of course none of this applies =
for straight grade oils.   Hope this helps.
