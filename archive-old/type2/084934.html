At 09:34 AM 5/10/02 -0600, you wrote:
>Hi Folks,
>
>I was thinking again (always dangerous) about helping the aircooled engine 
>run cooler and came up with an idea that I wanted to bounce off of The 
>List.  In lieu of spending up to $250 for an external oil cooler and 
>dealing with the mounting and thermostat issues, I was thinking about 
>alternatives that help keep the notoriously-hot T4 engine cooler.
>
>I was driving in the rain yesterday, and noticed that my engine temps were 
>much lower than they usually are on this same route.  I got to thinking 
>(see first sentence) about the cooling and evaporative effects that water 
>has on the aircooled engine, and wondered if the "rainy day" condition 
>could be replicated on dry days.  I live at 8,000 feet elevation, and the 
>air up here is very dry and pretty thin, so its cooling effectiveness is 
>not as great as it is in thick, humid air environs.
>
>My "rainy day" theory is that road spray on the bottom of the oil sump 
>adds more cooling, which makes the oil cooler.  Also, the increased 
>humidity (both from rain and road spray) gets pulled into the fan where 
>its evaporative effects add more cooling to the cylinders and oil cooler.
>
>I came up with the idea of a "water injection system" which goes something 
>like this:  A windshield washer reservoir on the left-side battery tray 
>with a low-pressure pump that could be actuated by a momentary switch on 
>the dash.  A hose runs from the reservoir to the tin in front (RIF) of the 
>fan, where water would drip onto the tin and get sucked into the 
>fan.  Water droplets would either flash into steam or get atomized by the 
>fan, get blown over the cylinders, and add humidity and density to the 
>cooling air.
>
>It all seems to work (in theory), but I'm sure there are problems with 
>localized cooling and different rates of metal expansion and contraction.
>Anybody care to jump in on this one?
