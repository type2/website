Test it again - you need to know that it's still okay.  Save yourself some
money and buy the compression tester.  It's a simple procedure and it's a
nice skill to add to your collection.

>>Test
>>for fuel by dripping some fuel in the right carb with a turkey baster or
>>straw while the engine is idling.  Do the cylinders suddenly start firing?
>>Then fuel is your problem.  Otherwise, it's spark.  See how easy that was?
>>:-)
>
>So you're saying I should remove the #1 plug at idle, drop some gas in the
>right carb, and look for hesitation. Repeat with #2 plug. Correct?

No.  Think about what you're trying to prove.  Your hypothesis is that at
idle, the engine is pumping (compression) and the plug is sparking (spark)
but there is no fuel entering the cylinder from the central idling system.
To test this, you will drip some fuel in through the throat of the carb,
which is a crude way of simulating the idling system.  Don't remove the
plugs because you still need them to fire for the test.

>I just don't see how it could be spark since I've replaced everything but
>the rotor -- and how could a rotor only effect two cylinders?

Excellent reasoning but not 100% conclusive.  Wierd things can happen with
spark.  Still I agree that it's probably a fuel problem.

>I did notice that the idle seemed high and she would bog down just about

See if you can still go 70.  Your mechanic may have retarded your timing
too much (reduced power) or adjusted your point gap too large (bogging at
