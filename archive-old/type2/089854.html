Take this with a grain of salt, but here is what I have found from a
reliable source, who works on nothing but air cooled Koehler, Wisconsin,
and Briggs, and Straton, engines--and big horse power stuff too---

He attends seminars, offered by these various manufacturers all the time
to keep up to date on engine data.

Now, he has told me flat out as I collect JD garden tractors, that every
mfg. has instructed the engine rebuilder's time in and time out, that a
multi viscosity oil is absolutely not made for an air cooled engine, and
nine times out of ten, and engine failure can be attributed to using
multi viscosity oils.

The reason is they just can't hold their viscosity under load, with the
operating temperatures that an air cooled engine develops--

Now OK--a VW Type 2 isn't a lawn mower engine,but they most certainly
develop mucho heat, just as an air cooled VW engine does, if not more
under extreme driving conditions in the heat of the summer months---

With this information in mind, I'm changing my thoughts about multi
viscosity oil--which up until a few years ago wasn't in my
vocabulary--until I forgot to lighten up my oil weight in my 67
Bonneville 428, at -20 below----the 40 weight must have been like
glue,as I had to heat up the oil pan for 3 hours with a charcoal fire to
get it running---

My error, not the oils---or the vehicles--

I have found that one of my tractors that I use often a  JD 140, which
has a 14 horse Koehler, which I was using 15-40 gobbled that oil up
cutting a 2 acre lot---I switched to straight 50 weight at 85--90
degree's here, and the consumption is just about zip---

My 3 cents---

The Bentley shows similar temperature ranges for 20w50 and 15w40. What's
the advantage of using 20w50? 
The numbers on a can of oil read as follows: the first number, usually
followed by a W is the viscosity at room temp (70 degrees fahrenheit)
the W means "winter" and says it will still pour at freezing temps. The
third number is the viscosity at ~250 degrees fahrenheit. If there is no
third number, then the first number is also the viscosity at 250. 
Thus a 20W-50 oil protects like a 20 weight oil at room temperature and
like a 50 weight oil at 250 degrees, a 15W-40 oil protects like a 15
weight oil at room temperature and like a 40 weight oil at 250 degrees.
An sae30W oil protects like 30 weight at room temperature and like a 30
weight oil at 250 degrees. An sae30 oil does the same, but may not pour
at freezing temps. I believe it is *possible* to find an SAE30 oil but
it is a challenge :-) 
Also, I asked someone about a single grade oil like 30 and 40 but they
said it has a hard time circulating.
