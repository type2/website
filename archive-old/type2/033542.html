It depends on what type of fuel system you have.  My knowledge of the bus
FI systems is a little thin, but I'll take a stab at it anyway:

Carbs cannot adjust for altitude and will therefore run richer as elevation
increases.  

D-Jet fuel injection is similar in that it cannot determine how thin the
air is.  Additionally, I would guess (no real world experience here) that
it would run even richer at idle in high elevation than a carb, since with
this system, fuel pressure varies inversely with manifold pressure, and
there will be less manifold pressure (approx. 1 in per 1,000 ft) at idle at
higher elevations.

L-Jet fuel injection has a mass-air meter which accurately measures the
amount of air going into the engine regardless of the elevation.  This
system will not run rich at high altitude like the previous two.

>From this, it looks like the L-Jet would be the only one that might benefit
from having the mixture enriched at high altitudes.  The D-Jet would need
to be leaned out, and there is nothing you could do about a carb short of
changing jet sizes.
