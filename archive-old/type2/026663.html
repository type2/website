	I must have joined the list after the post, I'll check the archives.
Based on yours and others comments, I have tentatively decided to replace my
isolator with this "smart relay" device.  The main thing is that I want the
full voltage from the alternator to fully charge the batteries and give me
nice bright headlights and I can't figure out how to boost the alternator
voltage (to compensate for isolator voltage drop) like I did on the stock
regulator/generator.  I had many happy years using the isolator with this
set up.

	I'm going to connect the smart relay between the alternator and the
aux battery to ensure that the cranking battery always stays fully charged.
The aux battery won't get any juice until the primary battery is charged to
cranking capability.  IMO it seems that a regular relay setup will allow the
charges to equalize between the batteries (even with a delay setting or
disconnect relay to allow the cranking battery to get you started).  This
shouldn't be a problem if you drive for awhile before having to crank the
starter again-both batteries will have time to recharge.  But if you drained
the aux overnight, then had to start and stop right away, you may not have
enough juice in the primary to start a still cold engine a second or third
time.  Has this happened to anyone?  It's also not good for the primary
battery to cycle that way.

	I'm planning to connect the crank battery to the ignition switch
circuit (ignition coil, brake lights, horn, wipers, defogger) and the aux to
everything else (interior and exterior lights, power outlets, pump, etc).
In case I drain the aux battery, and need to drive with headlights-very
likely-I am installing a pair of 50amp switches (protected by 50amp circuit
breakers) on the dash to allow me to switch from aux to primary battery
power to feed the fuse box.  (I have to be careful not to turn both switches
on at the same time or the equalizing amperage will surge through the
switches.)  I'm also thinking about rigging an idiot chime to alert me if I
leave the headlights on with the ignition off.  (I occasionally leave my
lights on and the aux battery goes dead.  It's very gratifying to be able to
start her right up!  Thanks to the isolator, the headlights work right
away.)  In case I have a low crank battery and a fully charged aux, I'm
thinking about a short jumper cable and battery switch to connect the two
positive terminals directly.  A volt meter on the dash will show the charge
for each battery.  OK David, or anyone, please tell me what you think of
this set up.
