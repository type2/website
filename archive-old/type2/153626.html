Jim,

I've never seen a Hall-effect electronic ignition fail because of dirt, dust,
grease, etc. I suppose that it's theoretically possible - just not at all
likely. Yes, very much sand with iron particles in it could definitely be a
problem, as the iron particles would stick to the magnet sleeve. But you'd
have to dump a teaspoon full in there. Because the Hall-effect units are
sealed in high-temp thermoplastic or epoxy, they're pretty much immune to
dirt, dust grease, grime, oil and moisture. And they operate in the dark - not
dependent on line-of-sight optics.

I'm not saying that the Crane unit is not good - I'm sure it works very well -
I wouldn't mind having one to play with, myself. If you want to spend the
money and time required, I'm sure it would make a great ignition system. But
with the Crane unit, you have to find a place to mount an external box and do
some additional wiring work. And the Crane unit costs about $100 more than the
Hot-Spark kit.

The Hall-effect units: Pertronix and Hot-Spark, have only a red wire and a
black wire that go to the positive and negative terminals on the coil,
respectively - simple, simple. No external box to mount. It all fits
underneath the distributor cap. You can use the stock distributor and, if you
want to for some reason, convert it back to points and condenser easily. You
can use a stock VW black Bosch coil, a Bosch Blue Coil (which isn't blue
anymore, but unpainted, I hear) or a high-voltage coil, as long as the
resistance in its primary circuit is at least 3.0 Ohms. Hmm.. I wonder if a
Hot-Spark unit could be used to trigger the Crane box?

Out of a couple of dozen or so Pertronix units, I saw one that failed, but it
was DOA, right out of the box. I saw several Compu-fire coils (the
"distributorless" variety - 4 coils on one board) fail. So far, I haven't had
a single Hot-Spark ignition fail.

I *do* think that we can agree on one thing, though: points suck. They're the
least reliable part of a vehicle (not including the driver). They go out of
adjustment within 1,500 miles or so and affect dwell and timing. Sure, it's
easy enough to readjust the points and then set the timing (yet again), but
why bother? I'd rather be driving my bus or working on something else.

      Ah, but it is NOT a "Hall" Ignition System.  Allison/Crane XR700 units
have been an infared-opto-trigger beam style for the past 30+ years.  The beam
is broken by the slots in the rotor disc installed on the distributor shaft
just below the ignition rotor.  The nice thing about the opto-trigger beam is
that dust/dirt does not usually affect it, unlike cheeper Hall "magnetic"
style ignitions.

      But it is still classified as an Electronic Ignition System.
