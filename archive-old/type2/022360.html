At 03:58 PM 8/30/00 -0700, Mike Dwyer wrote:

>At light throttle cruise you can run the full advance of 38 - 40 degrees
>with the mech / vac distributor.  When you get into a load condition or
>full throttle application, your vacuum falls to 0, or near 0, degrees
>and you are in effect using only the mechanical portion of your
>distributor which is engine speed dependent.

I want to add one thing to this, just to be clear.  With a dual vacuum distributor, the vacuum retard will have no effect at full throttle, and a varying effect at part throttle.  If you're measuring from a reference point of idle with the vacuum retard connected, at full throttle you will have *relative* advance supplied by both the mechanical and vacuum retard mechanisms (and maybe some vacuum advance - I'm going to test this today to remove all doubt - from my mind anyway).

>To fine tune a mech / vac distrib for your specific engine requirements
>and available fuel, you disconnect and plug the vac advance hose to the
>dist. at the carb.  Load the bus up to near its max weight, find a long
>steep hill and accelerate (uphill) in 3rd gear listening for ping.

Keep in mind that advancing your timing to the threshold of pinging won't necessarily give you the most power.  It may be that less advance will give you more power, and of course a greater "cushion" against pinging.  A chassis dyno is the ultimate tuning tool and it is not just for race cars.  I've had my bus strapped down twice now and I've found that I could retard the timing quite a bit with no loss of power.
