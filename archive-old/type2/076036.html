> On Sun, 25 Nov 2001, Mike Soultanian wrote:
>

> >
> > It's much different than the "run your engine at 2k for 20 minutes"
> > method...
> 
> Mike,
> 
> I think that it is only different in being more theoretical and less
> procedure oriented.  There are some differences, but they are minor and
> related to the different natures of the vehicles. The discussion of theory
> is solid (brake vs. break aside :-) and valuable for us to know.
> 
> Below, I compare Joe Fournier's procedure in the library to the NTNOA
> page:
> 
> >From Joe Fournier:
> 
>   1.Immediately upon starting, run engine up to 2000 rpm for 20 minutes.
>     Watch oil pressure and temp gauges. You're letting parts machine
>     themselves to each other for preliminary fit.
> 
>   2.Drain oil while it's hot. Replace before starting again. If time
>     allows, let engine cool and check valves.
> 
> >From NTNOA:
> 
>     An engine's initial run should be used to bring oil and coolant (air,
>     oil, and/or water) up to operating temperature only, with little or no
>     load, then shut down and allowed to cool to ambient temperature. This
>     is important. After each run the engine needs to completely cool down
>     to ambient temperature.
> 
> My comment:
> 
> Both agree on a no-load run.  To bring a properly tuned and cooled VW
> engine to full temp takes 20 minutes, so they match in that respect.  The
> time would be different for a bike engine which is smaller and has no
> forced air cooling. Really the only difference is the 2K RPM which is not
> spec'ed in the NTNOA article, but is implied elsewhere in the article by
> phrases like, "We must achieve a happy medium where we are pushing on the
> ring hard enough to wear it in but not hard enough to generate enough heat
> to cause glazing."  I'll bet an inquiry on initial run to those same
> experts would produce a recommendation of close to 2k RPM.
> 
> One flaw in Joe's article is the "if time allows".  Time *should* be
> allowed!!!  The NTNOA article is correct, IMHO.
> 
> Joe Fournier:
> 
>   3.Inspect drained oil for metal particles. React accordingly; check for
>   magnetic properties to distinguish aluminum and brass from iron.
>   Aluminum = bearings...some wear is expected, but no chunks. Brass =
>   timing gear or valve guides. Shouldn't find much of this, if any.
>   Iron/steel = crank, cam, rings, cylinders.._dot_definitely no chunks, but
>   I'd expect *some* of this in breakin oil.
> 
> NTNOA:
> 
>   Oil change comes much later, read on...
> 
> My comment:
> 
> This may be apples vs. oranges, the load on a <500 lb. bike is way less
> than a 1 ton bus.  The idea of "lightly and gently" for a bus is hard to
> imagine... put it in 1st and you are pushing a ton, no matter what.
> 
> Joe Fournier:
> 
>  4.Run engine for 5-10 miles doing the drive up to 55; then wind
>    (down-shift) down to 20 mph...at least 5 times...as many as 10. You're
>    letting parts further machine themselves to each other with the
>    emphasis being on the cylinders, which must machine themselves under
>    load because their position/geometry is slightly different at different
>    RPMs...thus, the variance in RPMs and the downshifting. I don't fully
>    understand this, but Bob Hoover instructed me to do this and I do it
>    religiously. I consider this as important if not more important that
>    the 20 minute breakin.
> 
> NTNOA:
> 
>   This time give the engine light loads at relatively low rpm and stay
>   out of top gear. Lugging the engine, i.e., low RPM with a lot of
>   throttle (manifold pressure), is more detrimental than high rpm. Another
>   key is too constantly vary engine load during the entire break-in
>   period. A constant load is not ideal for breaking in bearing tolerances.
>   This second run should last only 10-15 minutes before another complete
>   cool down.
> 
> My comment:
> 
> There are definite differences here.  Going to 55 (in 4th, I hope!) then
> slow down vs. light loads and stay out of top gear being the biggest, but
> I think this may be a motorcycle vs. car thing.  MC's have more gears and
> run at higher RPM's.  The times are, however roughly equivalent, if you
> match minutes vs. miles and the constant variation of load is the other
> vital point both articles make.
> 
> Joe Fournier:
> 
>   5.Let sit over night. Check valves.
> 
>     I record all valve settings (per valve) and maintain that information
>     for the first 300 miles. I check and adjust valves at the following
>     times:
> 
>      after 20 minute break-in
>      after 5-10 mile break-in
>      at 25 miles
>      at 50 miles
>      at 150 miles
>      at 300 miles
> 
>     If I notice trends, I check more often. Since work is about 25 miles
>     round trip, I usually get pretty close on these.
> 
> NTNOA:
> 
>   The third run should see slightly higher rpm with light to medium power
>   loading using short bursts of acceleration to help seat the rings. Again
>   10-15 minutes of running should do it and again avoid top gear. A forth
>   run should consist of light to medium engine loads with a few more
>   bursts of medium-high rpm, and lasting just 10-15 minutes varying the
>   engine load and again avoiding top gear. Next while the engine is still
>   warm drain the oil and change the filter. This gets out the new metal
>   particles that are being worn away. Most of the metal particles will
>   break away within the first 50 -75 miles. To ensure the rings seat well,
>   use the same high quality oil and don't be shy about short duration high
>   rpm blasts through the lower gears after the oil has been changed.
> 
>   A few more 15-20 minute sessions should be used to work up to the
>   engine's redline gradually increasing the engine loads. After some
>   definite hard running and 250-500 miles it's a good idea to check the
>   valves.
> 
> My comment:
> 
> Here again there are more similarities than differences.  VW's are very
> sensative to valve adjustment, Nortons may well be less so.
> 
> Note that the Norton's first oil change occurs at ~1-2 hours of run time
> rather than 20 minutes, but the initial oil change occurs rather quickly
> in either case. This is a point of contention among many people I have
> talked to.  Do you leave the initial oil and it's filings in to speed
> break-in or change it to slow it down... intelligent people I respect vary
> on this and I am not sure there is a "correct" answer.
> 
> In either case, you are building the engine up to "normal" operating
> conditions over a series of runs with frequent checks.
> 
> Joe Fournier:
> 
>   6.At 300 miles, pull the engine and retorque the heads. This is a step
>     many people skip, but I'm not inclined to. It will tell you a lot
>     about how your engine is running and is neccessary to assure engine
>     longevity and reliability. It gives you a chance to tighten up tinware
>     and do those things that you meant to do but were in too much of a
>     hurry to do the first time around.
> 
> NTNOA:
> 
> After 500 miles re-torqueing the head is suggested.
> 
> My comment:
> 
> Again, a slight difference in mileage, but not significant.
> 
> In closing:
> All in all, given the apples and oranges nature of the beasts, I would say
> that there are more similarities than differences and if someone told me
> they broke in their VW engine using the NTNOA procedure, I would certainly
> not have any problem with that.
>
