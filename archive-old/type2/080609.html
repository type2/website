> Hi Mike,
>
>     Try local pawn shops for a inexpensive "C" clamp type spring
compressor.
> To remove the valve guide: buy an 8 mm X 1.25 tap. Tap the top of the
guide,
> screw a bolt into the guide. Use a long drift, (flat on the end, not a
> pointed punch) from the bottom, and drive the guide out with a hammer. The
> trick here is to make sure the drift is hitting only the bolt, and not the
> bottom of the guide. If you hit the bottom of the guide it will "Flair"
the
> guide and spread the guide hole in the head, ruining it. Some heads,
having
> the guides replaced several times will loosen the holes to the point where
> you must use over sized "OD" guides. They only come in 1st and 2nd
oversize,
> after that replace the head.
>     Reinstall the guides by driving them in from the top. Use a bolt
again,
> a 6 mm this time with at least a 1/2 inch of the threads ground off (so
the
> threads don't damage the inside of the guide) the shank of the bolt. Place
a
> flat washer on the bolt so the head of the bolt will sit on a flat surface
> on the guide. You wont have to pound it in, just light taps should do the
> trick.
>     At this point you have the guides replaced. You can't just install the
> new valves though, the new guides will never go back in the same place, so
> the valves wont close in there seat. (they will be offset to one side or
the
> other) You will need to ream the new guides for valve fit, and machine the
> seats. The reamer is to size the "ID" of the guide to the valve so the
valve
> doesn't bind and freeze up. A reamer costs less than $20.00 at any good
auto
> supply house, but unless your ready to spend some big bucks on a hard seat
> grinder, I would take the heads to a machine shop for the guide reaming
and
> seat cutting. This is one place where you just can't cut corners.
>     Good luck, I hope this helps.
