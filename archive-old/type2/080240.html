Couple of problems right off the top of the heads: What would you use
for a heat exchanger that would survive being submerged in water.
Boilers (domestic) use a cast iron water jacket and a circulating pump
but the heat source is intermittant, controlled by the thermostat - On
an engine the heat is constant and would re- quire two "radiators", one
in the bus for heat ( a heater core ), and a secondary one outside to
remove the heat from the water. A water cooled car already has both. 
Electric water pumps are readily available but not cheap. Racer and
marine suppliers have plenty available. 
Electric fans would be needed at least on the heater core and probably
on the radiator, too. Placement of the (especially), outside radiator
would require forethought - airflow without being subjected to stones or
other road dangers. 
Plumbing - What kind of tubing/piping? Stainless steel? Copper? And
fittings to make turns/connections? Sweated connections? Rubber and hose
clamps? 
Expansion/overflow tanks? Water expands when heated. A domestic boiler
has a large expansion tank with an internal bladder to take up this
expansion and return it when cooling. A water cooled engine has an
overflow tank plumbed off the radiator, some even use an electric fan
after the engine has stopped to continue to cool the water. Provisions
for "air bleeding"...to prevent cavitation. Put two different metals
together in a water bath and you've created a primitive battery.
Dielectric connections? Sacrificial anodes? I tend to think upgrading
the air system at whatever cost would be much cheaper than trying to
engineer a water system from scratch.
