Ok,

I try to follow the list wisdom, as we [on the list] do things different.
We all tend to tweak and pull our engines a lot more than the VW factory
likely considered "average", so they thought you can deal with the damn
exhaust donuts once every 60,000 miles .. but we all tend to do things more
often, right :)

So I heard Ken and others rave about welding flanges on and then using a
gasket + two nut & bolts.

Yep, I'm sure that 98% who try this found it to be a piece of cake -- but
me, I seem to have trouble with everything.  I even enlisted the help of my
intelligent girlfriend to help me master the introduction of welding.
Bought a welder, this was non cosmetic work, so I figured it was good
learnin' time.

What I learned:

  -- The #1 issue is alignment, the two flanges must be in sync for the bolt
holes to line up.
  -- I believe Hoover and others recommend putting the muffler and heat
exchangers on the bus to do the welding.  I started by welding the flange on
the muffler, outside the car, then grinding it down to get a perfect seal.
However, to ensure alignment of the two flanges (hole to hole alignment), I
put it on the bus before doing the heat exchanger side.  This doesn't work
well at all on 1968 later busses with support bar.
  -- I also put a set on my new engine for 1963 truck, no support bar.  This
welding wasn't easy for me.  You are trying to weld a circular seam, not
cover the bolt hole areas, and well -- for me it isn't easy.  And also just
as difficult to grind the mistakes off :)  You have to get the weld right at
the seam where the pipe and flange meet, all the way around.
  -- Then of course, I consider that what if I want to swap parts -- gee,
what is the likely hood that any of my muffler / heat exchangers sets are
going to match alignment of the bolt holes :)

I love the idea, as I also hate the donuts, but using flanges just opened up
a new set of problems for me.

Perhaps if the flanges had a groove instead of a hole, they might work
better.  Don't heat risers have a "groove" instead of a hole where one side
connects?  Instead of just a small holes -- that must line up.  Of course, a
larger size flange would be a problem with the 1968 and later bus support
bar, wouldn't it?

Yep, I really have no talent, and I turned a simple design improvement into
leaking exhaust flanges that I can't remove :)  I will likely have to drop
the heat exchangers (and muffler) to take them out and properly grind /
weld.  Well, maybe not properly -- but better than I did trying it around
the engine support bar.
