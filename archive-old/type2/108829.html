I got to know some people in East Germany pretty well after the repatriation
of the two Germany's. Their attitude was "why bother"? They did what they
were told to do, or not to do, as the case may be. But there were so many
stories of people painting until they ran out of paint and walking away from
the job, or people driving a truck full of hogs until it broke down and
walking away from it (hogs died, pork wasted). Nobody cared. Nobody tried.
Why should they? The government provided the basics and there was nothing
more than the basics to be had. Camera shops with no cameras. No
supermarkets. I saw a "wanted" ad in a dog magazine for a dog brush. You
couldn't even buy a dog brush, and people made their own training collars
out of welded wire. Some of those people over there were so, well.. stupid..
that they were almost drooling on themselves. They didn't care about money.
Money had nothing to buy. Yes, they lived, but it was not much of a life.
And everyone was afraid of the Stazi.
On the other hand, engineering, industry, international commerce and yes,
Volkswagens did wonders for West Germany's recovery. They were becoming a
modern and affluent country again only 20 years after the end of a war that
nearly destroyed it.
Like I said, capitalism is simply the best way of spreading the love.
One of the bad things that has come of the Volkswagen in Germany is the fact
that there was such a really HUGE demand for these cars worldwide that

Germans tried very hard to be tolerant for many years, but it just seems to
go against their grain. Now, the Turks have long since settled down, not

costs of repatriation and rebuilding (The communists had done little to

It's all very interesting, really, and a case could be made for capitalism
vs. communism/socialism based on a comparative study of the two post WW2
