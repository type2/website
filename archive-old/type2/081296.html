> Having followed this thread from the beginning, I wanted to weigh in with
a
> few observations.  It seems to me that laziness, lack of money, lack of
> knowledge, and lack of vision all contribute to why people can't fix
things
> right.  Here's how I see it:
>
> Lack of Vision:  "I needed a car, and my neighbor had this old 1960s VW
Bus
> in his backyard, so I bought it for $200.  I don't know much about cars,
and
> I hear these things need a lot of work, but it runs.  I'll drive it as
long
> as I can, and buy something else when it dies."
>
> Lack of Knowledge:  "What do those red lights on my dash mean?  I never
got
> an owner's manual, and the PO said they were supposed to be on when the
> engine's on.  You mean I have to change the oil?  But it's air-cooled!"
>
> Lack of Money:  "Why buy a new wiring harness for $150 when I can buy a
> 100-foot spool of wire at Radio Shack for $10?  I'll just replace the
wires
> that need fixing, and it'll work okay until something else breaks."
>
> Laziness:  "I was going to fix the rust on my rockers last weekend, but it
> was raining, and the game was on, and then I got high, and by the time I
> woke up from my nap it was dark.  Besides, VW made these things pretty
good
> anyway, so I can still drive it and fix the rust next summer."
>
> If we're unfortunate enough to come across a Bus that has had POs with
these
> lines of thinking, those of us who care about these great machines can get

> frustrated.  On the other hand, if we understand what we're doing to our
> Buses (for better or for worse) and are up-front with buyers at sale time,
> we only have ourselves to blame for future bad VW karma.  I've been guilty
> of all of the above infractions (on all of my cars), but I'm only human.
>
> Best of luck to all who love, fix, and maintain these great old cars.
