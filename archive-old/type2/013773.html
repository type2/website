For all those who are too lazy to look on Tire Rack or wherever
I'll be more specific.  On a radial passenger (or reinforced or even
C load radial) tire this is how it works.

205/70R14 97

205 is width in mm.  70 is height of sidewall in percent of width.
R is radial.  It may be preceeded by speed rating which I won't
get into SR, QR, HR, VR, ZR, etc.  For a bus it is going to be R.
14 is obvioulsly rim diameter.  A number following all this is the
load rating the 97 here is something that can take about 1600
lb, I don't think there are hard and fast rules about how that is
listed.  If you can't get something that is a true C or "reinforced"
which is a European thing, a 97 or better (ideally 100 or more) is
what you want to be seeing.

So to get the total diameter of this tire ((2*205*0.7)/25.4)+14
the 2 is for 2 sidewalls top and bottom, the 25.4 is 25.4mm/inch
That gives a nominal 25.3" diameter.

Now for the excitement, that is SMALLER in diameter than the
stock 185R14.  Depending on you POV if there isn't a profile
number it is assumed that it is either 80, 82, or 85%.  I'm going to
assume 82.  The 185R14 ends up a nominal ((2*185*.82)/25.4)+14
or 25.9" diameter.

Anyway 205/70R14 is stock size on alloy wheels on Vanagons and
runs on steel rims just fine.  In fact a ridiculously large 27x8.5R14
tire mounts on those rims as well, just fine.
(truck tires are by diameter, width, rim size)

 I like the 205 on a bus or vanagon but it is also hard to find in a C
load rater.  The Tire Rack has a few choices in stock at the moment.
Other choices are 195/75R14 and even 195R14 but the latter is a bit
bigger than stock and might be just barely noticeable to the seat of
your pants.  27x8.5R14 is definitely noticeable but runnable on a
Vanagon and some fit on buses.  Which brings a final point.  These
are nominal specs and although they should mean something they
don't always when you get to the extremes.

But I'd bet any name brand 185R14, 195/75R14, 205/70R14, or 195R14
will fit just fine on a bus.  It is to also be noted although it isn't safe as
I always say I've run scores of passenger car tires that came on buses
for 100,000s of miles without issue.  If I put a set on, I put on reinforced
or C tires, but I've had no trouble with MOST passenger tires.  I've had
others that have blown out unexpectedly.  They do wallow like pigs.
I would never go run out and throw away a new set of passenger tires
that came on a bus or vanagon.  I'd inspect them frequently for bulging
uneven wear, etc and when it came time I'd buy good stuff.  These are
my personal opinions I know others have had bad experiences, so have
I but I've never lost control of a bus/van because of a blowout so I'm not
sweating it.  YMMV.

A final note is that in the specs listing for each tire, tire rack gives you
nominal diameter, widths at tread surface and max, revs/mile, etc.  Also
of course load range and may inflation.  There are a lot of good tires out
there that can take the load and have 44psi inflation pressures with
passenger style load ratings of 97 or 100.  These tires (the 205/70's here
mostly) go on big ole american cars that weigh more than your van, if
you got to choose something and can't order better, at least choose
those.
