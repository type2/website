When the engine is running, the relay ties the AUX battery into the vehicle's
charging system and in effect a second storage battery wired in parallel to
the primary battery.  Anything running off of either battery is in effect
running off of both .  The concern would be if rou were running so much
electrical equipment, the alternator could not keep up.  The AUX battery is
only isolated when the engine is shut off and the alternator is not producing
current.

>   I don't necessarily need the

Which is what it is designed to do.

A maually switched power switch to connect the batteries could be used in
place of the isolator relay.  That is the simplist way. A single on - off
switch to connect or interrupt the positive lead between the two batteries.
On when engine is running and every thing works.  Off when stopped, and only
those electrical components connected to the AUX battery function with no
drain on the Primary battery.  That is what the isolator relay does.  I
bought a bus once, and the PO had used a knife switch to accomplish this
function.  Worked really good, and I never burned out a relay.

Don't get me wrong, it just appears there is no problem in conflict with what
you want to achieve.
