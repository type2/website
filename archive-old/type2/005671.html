Pete:

For an engine to run it needs 3 things:

1) Spark, at the proper time.
2) Fuel, in the correct amount
3) Sufficient compression

So, the first thing you need to determine is, which one of the 3 are
you lacking.

Randomly swapping out parts in the hopes that you hit on the problem
can be very time consuming (unless you just get lucky early on) and
*very* expensive (if you're buying new parts to swap with.)

If you were just driving along and all of a sudden it just died, and
you *didn't* hear any expensive (i.e., mechanical,
metal-meeting-metal) sounds, then it's probably an ignition or fuel
problem, though it wouldn't hurt to run a quick compression test
first.  Look at your points (I assume you have them, not electronic
ignition) and see that they're properly gapped and that the timing is
at least roughly correct.  Inspect for loose or cracked wires, hoses
that might have come off or have cracks in them, check that the Temp
II sensor is tight in the head, etc., etc.

If nothing obvious turns up here (and your compression's OK) then pull
a spark plug wire, hold it (with pliers) next to a metal portion of
the engine, and have somebody try to start it up.  No spark?  Then
you've got a fuel problem that you need to diagnose.  Good spark?
Then you've got a fuel problem to diagnose.

You might need to learn more about your ignition system or fuel system
then you know right now, but both systems are fairly simple (a lot
simpler than you might suppose) and not that hard to trouble shoot if
you have the knowledge and a few simple tools.
