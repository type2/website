	A liquid boils, because its partial pressure exceeds the pressure it
is being subjected to.
	As you heat most (all) liquids, the partial pressure vs temp curve
is a smooth continumumumumum (SP?)
	Now the pressure the VW thermostat liquid is being subjected to is
caused by the bellows springyness 
	being pulled against by the spring trying to open the flaps. If you
heat it slowly, the partial pressure of the liquid will
	increase, and more liquid will evaporate until the pressure of the
gaseous phase is in equilibrium with the 
	partial pressure of the liquid. The change in internal pressure will
allow the bellows to change size, until it is in equilibrium
	with the flaps spring, which shold be pretty linear (Hook's law and
all), so a smooth  progressive response would be 
	what is expected.
	NOTE: The pressure in the thermostat is negative, and it is actually
sucking against the spring, using the difference
	between atmospheric pressure , and internal pressure  acting on the
bellows end caps, and pleats
	to provide the pulling force, but because the air pressure is
relatively constant, and the bellows geometry relatively constant
	this just serves to reverse the sign in the above explaination, and
it seemed easier to explain having it 
	push rather than pull.
