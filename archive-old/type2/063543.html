I guess I'll enter the melee. You may want to check out the oil faq
(http://ccwf.cc.utexas_dot_edu/~efrank/oilfaq.html). The oil FAQ states
"...There are six of the most important properties of a motor oil readily
available to the public: viscosity, viscosity index (VI), flash point, pour
point, % sulfated ash, and % zinc." Since we are concerned mostly about what
temperature the oil will turn into goo, pour point becomes one of the most
important factors for a winter oil.

The faq defines "pour point" as "... 5 degrees F above the point at which a
chilled oil shows no movement at the surface for 5 seconds when inclined.
This measurement is especially important for oils used in the winter. A
borderline pumping temperature is given by some manufacturers. This is the
temperature at which the oil will pump and maintain adequate oil pressure.
This was not given by a lot of the manufacturers, but seems to be about 20
degrees F above the pour point. The lower the pour point the better."

You can probably use 20W50 in your area with no worries about the oil being
to thick to pump, as long as it does not get below 10 F. Note that there is
considerable variation amongst brands of the same viscosity.
