NO NO NO!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!
I have blown a rear tire, on my folks' pickup truck, and didn't even swerve.
However, I know a couple people that have blown front tires, and gone swerving
madly across several lanes of traffic. One was lucky, and didn't hit anything,
the other person lost control completely. Think about it this way- when you
lose a front tire, one wheel slows down VERY quickly, which really pulls hard.
If it's a rear tire, all the momentum will continue to drag it forward, as the
center of mass is in front of the tires. the wheel with the blown tire will
slow down drastically, which can't spin the rear of the vehicle.Also, rear
wheels are fixed facing forward, while front wheels are free to turn. I would
heartily recommend not using questionable tires at all, but if you must, I
would say definately on the rear.

On Wed, 17 Jul 1996, H. Steven Dolan wrote:

> I was lucky enough to be given a nice set of bright YELLOW curtains to go
> with my bright ORANGE bus.  [..]   Befor I put the new curtains in, I 
>would like to wash them. [...]  I would like to know if anyone has had 
>experience with these or has washing recommendations?
>  
> How bad is the shrinkage problem?
> Are they colorfast?

Christa and I have washed the curtains in our westy twice in as many 
years, with very good results.  It sounds like we have the same curtains 
as you, right down to the colour.

To answer your questions:
1) We machine washed using Woolite (tm) in cold water, and tumbled dry at 
   the lowest heat setting, adding Bounce (tm) for that "Spring-fresh" 
   (tm) scent.
2) We did not notice any shrinkage, but then we didn't do pre- and post-
   wash measurements either.
3) We did not notice any fading.  In fact, they were remarkably brighter 
   afterwards.  Apparently, there was a *lot* of dust and junk in them when 
   we started!

As a footnote, we did notice some very heavy fading of the fabric in a 
vertical "stripe" pattern, suggesting sun fading over the years where the 
folds from the curtain rods exposed/shaded the material from sunlight 
coming through the windows.

In my experience washing the curtains is safe and beneficial.

Just my CDN$0.02.

I guess I was lucky (sic) to have owned a '75, since the pump is the one thing I
still use from that Westy.  I learned to hate early fuel injection on The Clone
(it was an orange pop-top, just like jillions of other ones, hence it was dubbed
The Clone by my next-door-camper at the 1982 US Festival in San Bernadino, and
the name stuck).  I really despised that fuel injection when the darn thing
wouldn't start every morning, but when The Clone met its fiery death, that was
the end of my fleeting romance with fuel injection and Bay Windows.  I bought
Sushine, my spartan '64 Westy with a hatch (in 1983) and have had a blissful bus
life since.  I just went and looked at my pump, and the one end plate that is
still on has a VW emblem (the plastic flex hinge gave up the ghost on the other
end), but on further inspection, it is actually a Coleman, though I didn't
notice if it is American or German made, but the cheap construction leads me to
believe that it is American origin.  The Clone also had the full size spare on
the nose, so at the time I owned it the purpose of the pump escaped me, since it
doesn't move enough air for anything but bicycle tires, or topping up full size
tires (its most common use).  Since the plug fits both the silver socket and a
standard cigarette, I can use it on Sunshine and on the Detroit junk my wife is
cursed with ('86 Mustang).

Happy trails,

I dis agree with both Bill Kennedy and Mike Wright.
Put the bad tires on the rack and get at least good used rubber!

I spun a Toyota 2WD truck into the center wall of westbound Hwy 360 during a
rain storm. Silly me, I thought I'd save a buck or two by not replacing the
rags on the back of the truck. I got caught in one of our semi-decade
downpours. When it rains in the desert it really RAINS! All of a sudden the
rear end flipped out in *front* of me. Hydroplane city! I don't need no new
tires because it NEVER rains in Phoenix. 
Famous last words, I thought as I slid BACKWARDS down about 300 feet of the
center wall with on coming traffic blinking lights and honking at me. What a
helpless feeling trying to perform all of the proper "skid" procedures as
your butt is puckering and time is slowing to a crawl. Somehow, all sound
ceased to exist as well untill the rasping sound of a D A grinder as the
truck ground against the concrete divider. The experience was not due to
"they dont know how to drive in the rain" but in this instance to the
inferior (nearly bald) tires. 
I will never again tempt fate with cheap tires. I advise that others value
their health (and the health of others) enough to do the same. Put the
things on credit or sell furniture before being a tire cheapskate!
