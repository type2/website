Jamie, David:

My experience is as a designer for an electrical engineering firm.

>in this example, the starter normally draws a large >amount of current, but the thin test light wire >won't allow it to pass, and the lamp is part of that >circuit.

The wire will do it's best to allow all that current to pass. The resistance of the wire (a constant in the equation related in ohms/ft) will generate heat as a by-product, melting the insulation of the wire and then possible arcing to other grounds.

>If you connect a dead battery to a fully charged one 
>using #12 wire, the wire will *not* burn up.

>>Under the right circumstances, I think it would be >>possible.  A dead battery has very low internal >>resistance.  If it's dead enough it will be
>>like a short to the charged battery.  Lots of >>current will flow.

Correct, current is determined by the load. Wire is sized based on expected loads, plus a safety factor.
It is NOT sized to limit current flow.

>insulation is part of the factors determining the >current capacity of wire.

Well, it's really the other way around. Isulation is chosen based on operating temperatures, duty cycles, location of the wire, etc. etc.

>The impedance will choke any high current effectively

No, I think that's not true. As I said above, wire is sized (in the National Electrical Code, anyway) based on expected currents, plus a safety factor.

>Actually,, most utilities DO not use breakers,,, or >fuses... none! Well,, that isn't exactly true

Not it's not. There's a circuit breaker before a step-down transformer, before a switchboard, before a house service panel, and usually at sub-panels as well, and always on individual circuits.

>that fuse does NOT provide any protection to the >secondary single apt breaker panel.

Breakers are to protect wire, not equipment. A panel is nothing but a can full of breakers protecting more wire. Wire is the weak link in this chain, and electrical engineering is a lot about protecting and sizing your wire and wire protecting equipment.

>even then, the #6 or #3 wires don't burn up with 400 >amps fault current

According to the NEC table 310-16 allowable ampacities for #3 and 6 wire at 75 degrees C is 100 and 65 amps, respectively. You're armchair engineering saying that those wires won't burn at 4-6 times their load rating.

>My point is, it is normal practice to ensure the >wires are long enough to provide enough impedance to >effectively choke and reduce the available
>inrush current to a level that the protection can >operate at.

I'm not familiar with this practice. I would assume that if you've seen cable coiled up in an enclosure, it's for future use/splicing/extention, whatever. That is a much more logical reason.
