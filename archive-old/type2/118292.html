On 11/03/03, you wrote:

> Here's the deal as I remember it:
>     If you remove the battery cable from either terminal of the battery
> and connect one lead of the volt meter to the battery (where you
> took the cable off) and the other lead to the disconnected battery
> cable, the voltage reading should theoretically be 0 . A reading of
> 12 volts will typically indicate a short. Once this test has shown
> that a short may exist, my next step is usually to remove one fuse
> at a time and see if any single fuse makes the volt meter read zero.
> If I find that one fuse makes the volt meter drop to zero, I know
> that the problem is in the circuit that the removed fuse was
> protecting. Hope this helps. P.S. Depending on which cable you
> remove and which lead of the volt meter you connect to the
> battery/cable, the polarity of your reading will change. Not a big
> deal for digital volt meters but with an analog meter this may be a
> problem.
