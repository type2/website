> Don't throw that non-functioning clock away. A lot of times the problem is a
> piece of low temperature solder that is used as a fuse. Once it melts or
> breaks the clock stops. The following website goes over the repair
> http://www.nateswaterart_dot_com/vw/vdo_repair/
>
> The website shows the clock totally taken apart with just the clock
> mechanics shown. One does not have to take the clock completely apart. Most
> fixes only require the removal of the back plastic cover.
>
> One other item that's not mentioned on the website that I found I had to
> correct was lubricating the adjustment knob stem seals front of the clock.
> If you look behind the front adjustment knob at where the stem passes
> through the glass, you'll see that there are a set of seals that keep dust
> out. My seals were so dry that they actually kept the clock mechanism from
> being able to move the hands of the clock. A couple of drops of silicon
> lubricant fixed this.
>
> These clocks are very robust mechanical devices. Many times nothing besides
> the solder fuse is wrong. I bridged the broken solder fuse with wire and
> plan to install an in-line microampere fuse when I put it back in my bus.
> Right now it sits in my kitchen while I tweak it so it keeps accurate time.
