As another poster noted, at high elevation the compresson is lower because
there is less air per unit volume.  I've also wondered if the fuel mixture
might become richer with less air per unit volume in a device that metes
out fuel by measuring airflow.

The symptom you notice is that bus gets very sluggish- loses power. John
Muir recommends advancing timing to compensate. Others have said this is
less good than doing some combination of timing adjustment and fuel mix
adjustment, but I don't know just how. The most recent round of posts seem
to mention only picking (usually increasing) the compression ratio.

Fuel mix and timing can be adjusted on an engine without much trouble. You
could do this for a trip to the mountains and undo it when back at sea
level, if necessary.  Adjusting compression requires rebuilding engine and
adding/ removing shims.  under the cylinders or swapping heads.
Impractical to do just for a high trip.
