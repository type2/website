TR Gesell wrote:

> Tom,
>
> The short answer is no, altitude will not affect vacuum gauge readings.
> However, as you already know, altitude affects engine power, so at a higher
> altitude, you will need to operate at a lower indicated vacuum than at sea
> level.  Lower vacuum = more air volume to the cylinders.
>
> Conveniently, for every 1000' of altitude, ambient air pressure (and
> manifold pressure) drops by about 1".  As a San Diego (sea level) bus
> operator, I try to limit my cruise and hill climb power to 5" of vacuum so
> I'm not "maxing out" my engine all the time.  But when I go over the
> mountains, for every 1000' of altitude, I lose some power, so I allow myself
> another 1" of vacuum to make up for it.  Example: Mountain Pass at 3000' I
> climb at 2" of vacuum. At 5000', I'm wide open (vacuum = 0"). If I can't
> maintain speed at 50 MPH on a hill using the appropriate vacuum, I downshift
> to 3rd.  The government tends to label altitudes on highways so you don't
> need a barometer.
>
> My method is a bit detailed, but hopefully you get the idea.  At 7000' you
> will probably be at 0" vacuum to climb hills anyway, but if you drive down
> to the flatlands you can use your vacuum gauge to manage your power.  It
> also is a good trend tool for general engine health.  My typical readings
> are:
>
> 77' Westy 2.0L FI, Sea Level
> Cold Idle:  10-12"
> Hot Idle:       17-21"
> Cruise at 60 MPH:  10-11"   (Head/Tail Winds can make a BIG difference here)
> Climb at 50-60 MPH:  No Less than 5", adjusted for altitude.
>
> My idle vacuum and RPM's do vary with air temperature, since my cooling
> hardware is a work in progress.  I suspect when I get a thermostat it will
> idle consistently around 19". Have fun with your vacuum gauge!
>
