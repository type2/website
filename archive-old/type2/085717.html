Matt,
Just to shoot a temp related snake you should measure the resistance of Temp 
Sensor 1 ( You replaced Temp Sensor 2). Temp Sensor 1 is located at the 
input of the intake air sensor. What year is your Bus? From Feb 1976 on the 
connector air sensor has 7 pins. the resistance at room temp between pin# 6 
and pin# 27 should be around 2500 ohms. If yours is earlier model with 6 
pins measure between pin# 6 and Pin# 9.

Some recommend finding vacuum leaks by spraying WD9 or propane at a 
suspected leak and noting any increase in RPM or if they have a C0 detector 
looking for a jump in C0 in the exhaust.


Chuck

>Matt
>I think that you have a major leak. Low level leaks would have to be 
>numerous to cause such a condition on their own. I have had ground strap 
>(battery to chassis) that caused all sort of weird symptoms. Just last 
>month a rusty strap caused starting problems. As I wiggled it it would go 
>too a few ohms. A big ugly NAPA strap fixed that problem. A poor strap from 
>the tyranny to chassis can cause the clutch cable to fail I am told. Seems 
>conduction through the cable causes metal migration problems.
>
>Its hard to work in the cold.  Hunting for vacuum leaks has been a way of 
>life for me and my 77.  I have to believe leaks are problematic in the 
>older FI models.
>
>You can adjust the Air Fuel Mix with the hex screw on top of the Air Flow 
>Measurement box. Turn it clock wise until it hits bottom (count the turns). 
>This is as rich a it can go. Hook up the sensor and see if it will run. 
>This mixture adjust inter acts with the idle adjust screw so the idle will 
>most likely be off. But if this lets it run with sensor hooked up it points 
>to a vacuum leak.
>
>In this case go to the Type2 search engine and type Vacuum leak for a 
>wealth of info.
>
>Good luck and let me know what you find.
>Chuck
