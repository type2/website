I'll second those observations. My field notes from the luvbus' honeymoon
cruise through the Rockies with my luvin' wife back in '95 include the
following: VERY noticeable loss of power at 9000' feet in N. New Mexico.
Based on inference from things written by John Muir, I advanced the timing
a few degrees (about 5, from 7.5 BTDC to 12? I forget) in an attempt to
compensate for altitude. Also switched to 87 octane gas (that was before I
learned that US and Euro octane measures are different and I thought I
needed to burn 91 octane to make my bus happy).

And then one fateful morning while charging up a steep Forest Service road
in the Wind River range, my crankshaft broke clean in half. I hate it when
that happens! Not only grenaded my engine, but also my torque converter
and automatic tranny. Our beloved bus gushed red ATF like a whale bleeding
grievously to death. A low compression test at first led me to believe I'd
burned valves and left me scratching my head when I got in there and the
valves looked fine. Mail ordering parts and rebuilding the engine in a
remote wood shed was a tale of triumph and failure, but that's another
story. If I knew then what I know now...

A number of people have told me that the crankshaft was "just one of those
things," unrelated to the altitude or timing. But you're saying otherwise?
Just what is it about low compression plus high revving that might grenade
an engine?
