Subject: Fixing Gasoline Smells

Here's a procedure that I used to solve gas smell
problems after a fill up.  In my case the tank
grommets and hoses weren't that old.  If your tank
rubber is ancient you may want to replace that first. 
I sealed the leaking left grommet identified by the
test described below. When I first dropped the tank
years ago, the Y plastic connectors were really tough
to get out of the grommets on the top of tank.  I was
glad not to have to try prying these out by feel (does
anyone have any tricks for getting these  Ys out of
the grommets?)

In order to seal the leaking Y/grommet junction, I
figured it was critical to find something that stuck
well and resisted gasoline.  I remembered a stiff
putty I had called green wax that was impervious to
gasoline. I don't even know where to get it today.  I
sealed all around the junction of the Y connector and
the tank grommet with this sticky green wax.  Because
very few compounds stick well to the plastic of the Y
connector or to the rubber grommet, I covered the Y
connector with RTV.  I used several applications to
build up a dome that stuck to the tank itself, and
encased the Y, green wax, and grommet.  The hardest
part is you can't see what you are doing, and RTV
tends to be messy when you are applying it only by
feel.

The compounds I tried that resist gas are RTV (once it
is cured), green wax, and J.B.Weld.  I would have
probably used J.B. Weld if I could have seen what I
was doing.

Fill ups now are free of gasoline smells, so I hope
this seal lasts a long time.  By the way, the seal
passed the leak down test, indicating the value of
this test for trobleshooting gas smells.
