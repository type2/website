Not on a pick up, and they have identical cooling systems. The later 
1600 Baywindow pick up sucks air for the carb through the right underbed 
chassis member, which is connected to a vent/grille on the right rear 
corner of the cab.

>Moreover, the cross sectional area of the air 
>scoops is considerably larger than the cross sectional area of 
>the carb and the engine fan intake combined. The pressure of the engine 
>compartment at any given instant will be some function of rate of air in

My single cab has proven that there is airflow from under the tinware into 
the engine compartment. The engine bay is pristine shiny Grand Prix Weiss.
The (my) exhaust systems leaks, the openings where the heatrisers for the 
stock manifold protrude through the tinware are open. While driving around 
town, no problem, but after a small trip down the highway it has black root
deposit patterns, to call it that, on the insides of the rear fender wells, clear 
evidence of airflow from under the tinware through the holes, along the 
carbs, passing the fenderwells, hitting the front 'fire' wall, and into the fan 
and carbs, there are blacks streaks on the fresh white paint, it almost looks 
like an experiment to show the magnetic line around a magnet using iron 
powder. The engine room only gets dirty if I take it onto the highway. If there 
was positive pressure in the engine compartment it would be less critical to 
seal the engine compartment, other than to keep the pressure in there..  I've 
never been advised, or read about sealing the engine compartment so as 
to maintain the proper pressure at speed. It would also mean that by going 
uphill at low speed, with a considerable load, that the cooling system does
not reach its full capacity because the car is not travelling fast enough for 
proper cooling of a hard working engine.

>If there were negative pressure in the engine compartment at speed, as you
>assert, then I would expect engineering solutions that put more cool air in

These were 'popular' aftermarket items, Kamei made them in the past, 
for Bugs, Buses, VW used them on the Thing/181, and the have been 
copied by many fiberglass 'specialists'. The thing is, that there is almost 
no way that you can force more air through the stock fanshroud, that 
would take a considerable amount of pressure, and might even require 
special mods to the carb(s) (if VW would have designed it) the gearing 
is the variable that can control the ratio between cooling capacity/amount 
and engine load. I've read about testing being done to increase fanspeed 
by increasing the pulley size, the engine ran hotter because the fan 
required more Hp to be ran at that speed, but it did not flow more air 
through the stock system. (I've read it a while back, relying on memory). 
A plus for an overpressure engine compartment would be that it would 
make up for possible small leaks in the tinware and engine compartment 
seals.
