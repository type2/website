Jim Mowreader wrote-

>This takes some practice, but it's doable. It's almost a soldering job.
>
>You start by adjusting your flame down to the barest minimum you can get it
>to. Then you point your flame so it's not pointed at the bus, but so you can
>get it to the bus just by turning your wrist. Pointing up or pointing down,
>it works either way. You will have the MIG wire in your left hand and your
>torch in the right.
>
>Next, heat the MIG wire almost to the melting point. This is why you need a
>very gentle flame; MIG wire melts quick. When it's almost ready to melt,
>touch it to the ding and bring the flame to it. The wire will melt and fill
>in the ding, pulling a bit of the base metal in along with it.
>
>I don't quench because you're only heating the base metal for a couple of
>seconds anyway.
>
>I have a feeling you could do the same thing with lead-free solder, but in
>that case the technique would be different--stick the end of the solder in
>the ding and heat it until the solder melted.
>
>(Ding = hailstone dent, de-rusted rust spot, whatever you have that's not
>big enough to be a genuine dent.)

This is what i do now, although i have been using fluxed brazing rod
to fill small pinhole rusty ares too thin to mig but also too small to patch.
But then you are left with having to sandblast out the hardened flux.
At least it won't rust.
