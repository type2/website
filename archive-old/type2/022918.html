>Hmm. I think I can tell you why. Quite some time back there was
>discussion of selling cds of the archives, to fund the list. Quite a few
>folks pointed out that A: they owned the rights to what they had written;
>and B: they hadn't given anybody permission to sell them for financial
>gain-even for the non-profit support of the list. As I recall, Bob Hoover
>was one of the louder ones of the bunch-and his comments were arguably
>some of the most valuable. The fallout of all this was the removal of
>Hoover's sermons from the t2 website. Not because Hoover didn't trust
>Ken, but rather because other websites were stealing the content and
>providing it without attribution. At the time, the general consensus was
>that there was no way permission could ever be gained from all of the
>persons whose work would be in the cd. Since the job of trying to get
>permission from all of these people was not possible, there were two
>options. 1. publish it, and risk being sued or some such on down the
>line, or 2. don't do it. Obviously the choice at the time was #2.
>
>I don't think it's anything personal. I'd say it's more a case of Ken
>trying to cover his ass in an extremely litigious society. Because of his
>historical involvement with the list, were the archives published in some
>sort of non-free form (I know your time and DB skills aren't free, but
>you're not charging visitors for them, that's the key), Ken would likely
>be named in a lawsuit. As for the issue of them being an online
>searchable archive, I'd expect it's ok because of the precedent set by
>internet newsgroups. When you post to a public place, you are assumed to
>know that your posts could be included in a free online collaboration.
>
>Disclaimers: IANAL, and my recollections could have some factual errors,
>as I believe all of the events I'm mentioning occurred something like 5-6
>years ago. I do however think the above is relatively close to historical
>fact, close enough to gain a general 10,000 foot view...
