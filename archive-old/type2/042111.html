> Wires in a vibrating object like a car are generally of the stranded type,
> presumably because solid wires are prone to breaking. However when you start
> soldering them you actually turn them into a solid wire. The soldered end
> will become brittle and is a probable source of breakage. Actually the
> crimp-on connectors are not so bad. The trick is that you have to apply
> proper pressure when crimping them on. In the factory they will use heavy
> duty tools and connectors that correctly match the wire diameter. 

You're not a'kidding there!  The factory crimps are done under huge
pressure.  If you carefully remove a factory-crimped connector you will
find the copper wire underneath to be bright and shiny, just as it was
when new.  I don't believe that you can get this level of protection with
consumer-grade crimping tools, so soldering is the best way to avoid
corrosion and loosening problems.  I've seen quite a few failures of
home-crimped
connections due to corrosion and loosening, and I'm a desert dweller.

I've never had a soldered connector fail, but no connector should be under
constant stress.  IN high-vibration/motion situations, the wire should be
supported on both sides of the connector, whether the connector is crimped
or soldered.

Also the
> isolation on the crimp-on connector supports the wire so it will not break
> so easily. Back in tech college I was taught the following procedure for
> stranded wires that have to be screwed down in plugs, terminal blocks,  etc.
> 
> - Remove about an inch (2.5 cm) of isolation.
> - Hold the soldering iron at the end of the stranded wire and let the solder
> flow so the strands are soldered together.
> - then cut off most of the soldered end leaving only about 1/8 of inch (2-3
> mm) soldered.
> - screw it down on the unsoldered part.
> 
> Two reasons for this. As I said, the soldered end is brittle and likely to
> break just at the point where it leaves the terminal block or whatever. And
> over time the solder will 'flow' under the pressure of the screw causing the
> connection to loosen.
> 
> So I have no hesition in using crimp-on connectors. But make sure you use
> the right diamter for the wire and crimp them on with all the power you have
> in you. Use proper tools, makes life easier (ever tried to use one of those
> plastic tools to mount an RJ-11 telephone plug and you know what I mean).
> 
> One more note: unsoldered connections can actually be better than soldered
> ones. I found that hard to believe when in the seventies wire-wrapping came
> into use in the computer industry. But the electrical connections made with
> wire-wraps had a lower resistance then soldered connections. But here again
> the procedure must be carried out with the proper skills and tools.

Wire-wraps were made by tightly coiling a light wire to a square pin.  The
corners of the pin made good low-resistance connections with the wire,
since there were dozens of contact points under high local pressure.
However, note that wire-wrap connections were usually not subjected to
harsh conditions.  If they were, they would loosen and corrode easily.

It is important that there be a good mechanical connection before you
apply solder.  Solder is not a good conductor, but it is a good "holder"
and sealer.
