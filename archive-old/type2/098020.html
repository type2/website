However, the aluminum covers are quite a bit thicker than the steel.
thickness reduces the heat transfer by conduction.  There's more to it
than the material.  If you wanted the ideal situation here, you would make
the covers of thin aluminum, anodize it black, and form ripples in the
material.  The result would be efficient, but fragile and hard to form.

Another consderation is the medium transferring the heat to the
dissipating surface.  The valve covers are not full of oil and in fact are
pretty much empty except for a oil fog and droplets flying around inside.
This is not really a good situation with which to transfer heat from the
oil.  You can't transfer heat from the head, either, because the gasket is
an effective insulator.

If you want to cool the oil effectively, you need to get the oil as a
liquid and then put it in contact with an efficient heat exchanger.  This
is why they went to the trouble to increase the surface area of the oil
sump with finning.

If the goal is to lower oil temperatures, a much better cost/benefit
ratio would result from modifying the existing heat exchanger (oil cooler)
for increased capacity than with alterations to the valve covers.
