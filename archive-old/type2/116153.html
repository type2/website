Subject: Recent Journey - kinda long

Well,  I finally found and purchased a high roof camper.  It is a '76 
and I like it a lot.  I had quite an adventure bringing it home and 
thought I would share it.

I saw an ad for a high roof on the samba.  Worked out the details and 
bought a one-way ticket from Denver, Colorado to Oakland, California 
where the bus was living.  The bus looked good, was a little loud from 
an exhaust leak but I didn't have any concerns about driving it the 
1200 miles home.  I would like to say that it went off without a hitch 
but I was taught not to lie so it would be a problem.

By the time I was past Sacramento the bus was dropping a pretty big 
puddle of oil at every stop.  It seemed weird because the bus ran very 
well.  So while at a rest area I poked around a bit.  I found that the 
aftermarket oil coolers had some loose clamps and some oil was getting 
by.  I managed to get them tightened up and things looked like they 
were improving.  A side note:  Part of what complicated my adventure 
was that I had no tools with me.  All I had was a stubby screwdriver 
with interchanging bits and my leatherman micra.  I thought about 
bringing some tools but it seemed like a big hassle given airport 
security and what not.  I brought what I had by accident and ended up 
having to check a bag to bring them.   So, back to the adventure.

I got into Reno, Nevada and noticed my tail lights were out.  Took them 
apart at the gas station and cleaned up some contacts and they were 
working again.  I stopped for the night just outside of Reno and slept 
in my high roof for the first time.  The next morning it was clear and 
bright, I had slept much longer than expected but I guess I needed it.  
I stopped for gas in Winnemucca just after lunch time and had a 
sandwich.  All was going well but the gas shut off didn't and I had 
some gas leaking on both sides.  I let this subside and cleaned up.  I 
came back from the restroom having degreased myself for the 10th time 
and jumped in to get back on the road.  I turned the key and nothing, 
zip, nada.  Seemed like I had plenty of electricity so I crawled under 
the car to take a look at the starter.  The PO told me he had just 
replaced the starter and it looked clean/new.  My stubby screwdriver 
was just long enough to short the terminals and it was alive!  I turned 
it off and then started it with the key.  Made sure I parked on a hill 
from then on.

Things went well until I was leaving Salt Lake, Utah.  I was about 20 
miles from Evanstan, Wyoming when the alternator light came on.  Oh 
well, I thought.  Good thing I have that type IV motor so I can just 
run the lights off the battery and fix it in Evanstan.  The PO had an 
extra belt but as I said I had no tools.  I got about 5 miles  from 
Evanstan when I remembered that fuel injected cars need more 
electricity to run.  My lights were dim and I was waiting for the 
engine to misfire.  I drafted in behind a semi (Thanks Watkins Truck 
lines :) and made it into Evanstan.  The bus died half a  block from 
the gas station and I coasted in.

I checked the belt and it had come off, not broken.  I looked around 
but didn't see anything open (It was after midnight) so I called upon 
the gods of improvisation.  I was able to get the belt about a third of 
the way on the alternator pulley.  I put the bus in second gear (on 
flat ground) and held the belt on the pulley while pushing on the 
engine.  I got lucky enough that it rode the rest of the way around.  
It was on, too loose for my taste but I had no tools.  I was able to 
push start the bus and get going again.  I was about 100 miles from the 
middle of nowhere (you know the place) when the alternator light came 
on again.  I stopped and checked and the belt was off.  I tried my 
trick again but it took a lot longer on the side of the road with a 
flashlight between my teeth.  Got in to start it up and nothing again.  
Pushed her again and got it going.  I gave up for the night about 100 
miles from Laramie.

In the morning I checked things over again and bought some gas.  I was 
kind of frustrated and very tired.  I was checking the oil and making 
sure nothing else was going wrong when an older gentleman walked over 
and commented "That's the problem with those Volkswagens, you have to 
work on them constantly".  He was gone before I could even come back 
with "nuh - uh!" I was able to drive the rest of the way without 
incident and had some time to think.

I was drained by the problems but impressed by the fact that with very 
little in the way of resources I was able to make repairs.  I know that 
was one of the original design concepts but it really came home on this 
trip.  Sure, it would have been easier with my normal "in the car" set 
of tools but I have that personal satisfaction that comes from 
surviving an ordeal.

Sufficiently bonded with my new bus I look forward to many more 
adventures.

Dave
