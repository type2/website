> Michael,
> 
> First, on controlling the voltage, the amount of time the points are open
> determines how long electricity feeds to the coil and condensor to
> "charge" them.

The coil is "charged" while the points are closed.  Current flows through
the coil to the points and ground, building up a magnetic field in the
coil.  When the points open, the magnetic field disappears.  The changing
magnetic field induces a voltage in the secondary winding of the coil, and
the large number of windings in the secondary creates a very high voltage.

> Like all systems involving a condensor/ capacitor, if you
> hook it to DC, it charges and then sits.  You have to discharge it and
> reload it to get current flow.  The rating of the condensor/capacitor
> determines how much it can store.  As I understand it, when the points are
> open, the electrons flow into the condensor, which stores them until the
> points close.

The purpose of the condenser is to save the points from erosion due to
high current switching.  When the points open, there is a fairly large
current flowing through them from the coil.  The condenser is a capacitor
that provides an inital low resistance to this current, preventing major
arcing of the points.  If the condenser goes bad in an "open" state, you
will go through points at a fearsome rate.  The condenser does not provide
energy to the coil, however.

There is such a thing as a "capacitive discharge" ignition wherein the
energy from capacitors is directed to the coil, but this is a different
setup.
