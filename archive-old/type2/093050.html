My take on "reliability" is that if the vehicle gets you where you're going,
when you want it to, then it's "reliable."  If parts are replaced when those
crafty Germans say they should (DM), then they are most unlikely to fail
under use.

A friend of mine refuses to consider an AC VW, as he claims they are
"unreliable."  He, it seems, is in the *unique* situation where he MUST
get to work on time everyday, or his boss will get mad.  So, he buys a
new Saturn every three years.  One day, his two month old Saturn
would not start.  Since he doesn't know how to open the hood, he had
to have it towed to the dealer, got a loaner, didn't make it to work that day.

After five days of scrutinizing the printouts from the computerized analysis
equipment, they still couldn't figure out the problem--so they sent the 
readouts
back to the factory.  After a couple of days, the factory folks hadn't 
figured out
the problem either.  They suggested that the dealer's mechanics <gasp>
PHYSICALLY look at the car to see if the electrical gremlin could be discovered
in this way.  Within only a few hours, they discovered that a mouse had eaten
through the ignition wire going to the coil.  Only took eight days for him to
get his car back.

If this had happened to my bus, it would have taken me five minutes to
discover the difficulty, and perhaps another five to correct the problem--
probably even make it to work on time.

So which car is more "reliable"?
