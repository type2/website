If the voltage stabilizer is bad the gauge should be pretty unstable at
all positions.
If it works ok in some places but gets jerky at specific spots then I
would expect it to be the sender.

> >The problem is intermittent connections in the sender.
> >Inside the sender are 2 stretched conductive wires and a contact on a
> >float the wires can get contaminated spots that dont conduct well and you
> >get a jerky fuel gauge.
> >The best fix is to replace the sender.
> >You can fool with the senders innards but its a real hassle to put it back
> >only to find that it still doesnt work so well.
> 
> I had a fuel gauge that worked like this, and I thought I needed replace the 
> sender. I knew that the sender was alright when I put it in, although it had 
> been sitting for a few years before I started using the car. I thought it had 
> gone bad. I was surprised to find that it worked just fine after I installed new 
> gauges. If you have a spare fuel gauge, or a friend who has one, you could 
> try and hook it up to your car and see if it will solve the problem, just to 
> make sure it is the sender. The fact that your gauge is 'jerkier' when the 
> engine is running could point to a faulty voltage stabilizer, the square thingie 
> behind the fuel gauge.
