On Sat, 24 May 2003, Access Systems wrote:

> well at least according to Mobil, the synthetics always flow at the lower
> number and always lubricate at the higher number and do not change
> apparent viscosity as temperatures change.

I think that Mobil is using  a little marketing magic here.  If the
viscosity did not change, the viscosity would not be the conventional
numbers that they are using.  It would be something weird like 30W20
indicating a flat viscosity/ temperature curve.

> my experience with synthetics is that there is an ever so slight thinning
> as the oil gets above 220degrees based on the combination of readings from
> my oil preasure and temp guages

The viscosimeter doesn't lie, and if the synthetic oil has a viscosity
index of, say, 10W50, it would have to have a viscosity/temperature curve
similar to a petroleum oil of the same index (Castrol used to make a
petroleum 10W50, IIRC).  Outside of the cold and hot test
temperatures, the curves may differ, but we try not to run our engines in
those conditions if we can avoid it.
