Hi Tim,
Yes, the pressure for a tire to wear evenly depends upon the weight it is 
carrying most of the time. And the sticker that comes with the vehicle is 
based upon assumptions as to the tire size it comes with and how much weight 
it might carry in the future. Usually the manufacturer of the vehicle errs 
on the size of conservatism, tending to overinflate (for example assuming a 
maximun load on board) so as to avoid overloading the tire and causing 
blowout (except, witness the Explorer where the recommendation was on the 
liberal side so as to lower the center of gravity by means of softer tires, 
resulting in some blowouts). There are tables of the maximum load a tire can 
carry at a given pressure, but I'm not sure if the result of using a tire 
and weight at the recommended pressure will result in even wear of the 
tread. So I think the best way to go is to observe how one's tires are 
actually wearing over the miles, and adjust accordingly.
My experience is with a 75 Westy which came with the 185-14 reinforced 
Michelins, still has them, and is recommended on the sticker to be 30/40. 
When I looked at the weight carrying capability at those pressures, I found 
that given the weight of the Westy and its distribution, adding my weight 
(200 lb) and my wife's weight (which will go unreported), that the weight 
carrying capacity was mush more then what I would subject them to viturally 
all of the time. Even at 26/34 I am well below the weight carrying limit. So 
get the weight and distribution factors for you van, figure your weight and 
the weight of stuff you typicaly carry, read the tire table, and act 
accordingly, Then observe what is happening to the tread.
Roland
