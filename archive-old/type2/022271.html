> >Rob:
> >
> >I don't think you need any sort of "high heat" type product for the
> >tinware since it never should really get that hot.  The big issue for the
> >tin is rust, which you address by a good cleaning and a good painting
> >(with ordinary paint.)
> >
>
>I agree. Use a small hammer to tap out the kinks and bumps. Hit the rust
>with abrasives. Use about a 220 then a 400 dry then 400 wet to smooth the
>whole surface and taper the original paint edges. Treat the bare metal with
>Ospho (chandelry) or other rust conversion chemical. Get Rustoleum or
>Krylon or other quality brand paint. Two cans oughta be plenty for the all
>the tin. One is probably enough. Give it three or five or, if your havin'
>fun, seven medium-light coats. Do one side, flip them then do the other,
>five to ten minutes between coats. If you get
>distracted and wait more than 20 minutes from the last coat, stop. If you
>go any longer than that it will turn into a wrinkle finish. (Ask me how I
>know.) Let it dry completely at that stage before continuing with the
>following coats.
>
>If you want the concourse look. Strip completely to bare metal to start.
>Use two or three medium-light coats. Let it dry completely. 400 dry, two or
>three coats. Dry competely, 400 wet, then a medium coat for the last. The
>concourse tech can take several days for drying between coats in cold and
>humid conditions. Itermediate coats should be hard dry before sanding. In
>hot dry conditions you might be able to get that down to a day in the sun.
>
>The only thing better is powdercoat.
>
