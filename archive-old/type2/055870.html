I wanted to thank all the folks who wrote in with advice on my 
question about towing a breadloaf.  For those of you who missed it, 
the advice fell into the following 4 categories:

1. Put in on a trailer or you face disaster.

2. Keep all four wheels on the ground or you face disaster.

3. You can tow it on a two-wheel dolly, but it must be backwards or 
you face disaster.

4. You can tow it on a two-wheel dolly, but if it's backwards you 
face disaster.

Well, I was planning to go with #4 but as it's the end of the 
semester in this University town, none were to be had during my 
window of Maximum Free Helpers.

So I got to thinking.  Beetle tow bars don't work on busses due to 
the reach involved (and torsion tube diameter?).  I was wishing my 
bus had a nice tubular front bumper that a beetle tow bar would fit 
on.  So I made one:

Two pieces of angle iron or the like (I used 2x3" aluminum box 
'girders') about 18" long, bolted to the front bumper mounting holes 
in the frame.  (You gotta remove the front bumper.)  The metal pieces 
stick out just far enough to clamp my 3' , thick-wall, 2" ID cheater 
pipe to them with some U-bolts.  So you've got a rig that looks sort 
of like a push-bar on a dragster.  The Beetle tow bar clips onto this 
and due to the space between the angle iron, it can't slide off 
either end.

We used this contraption to tow my $200 71 westy about 45 miles, 
doing around 35-45 the whole way, with no problems.  We 
would have gone faster but I was concerned about the running gear on 
the bus -- not the tow rig.  (No probs there - the right rear 
hubcap was hot to the touch at one pullover/checkit stop, but not 
really bad.)  The bus tracked very well the whole trip.  It's now 
resting comfortably in a barn in South Georgia, waiting for my 
ministrations.

SO I'm wondering if I should patent the "adaptabars" ;-).   Total 
cost was about $20 (for hardware and BIG drill bits), but I did have 
the bars and pipe already.

YMMV,
