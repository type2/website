BTDT.  Joe's $400 is low in my opinion.  Those of you in NEATO,
may have read my account of restoring a '67 Deluxe.  I put $350
in paint alone (with a "brother-in-law works there" discount).
That does not include, as Joe rightly points out as "prep costs".

Filler, sandpaper, grinding discs, primer, surfacer, sealer,
hardeners, putty, solvents, tack cloth, visqueen, gun cleaner,
etc. etc. etc.  This covers most of it *if you are going to do it
right*.  Certainly the Maaco approach can provide some salvation
for the person who doesn't have the facilities.  The recurring
theme in my dissertation on painting I stole from a paint
company:

Prepare the surface, insure the result.

Painting over crap is like building a house in a swamp.  It will
look good for a while.  It's up to you to determine how long you
want that *while* to be.

My approach was slow and easy.  I did all prep work, applied a
coat of color-coat and let it sit all winter without trying to
assemble anything.  I figured that if *anything* looked bad the
next spring I had not done some prep correctly.  The next spring
I then wet sanded the color coat and applied a final coat.  Four
years later I have one small bubble on the nose and one in a
rocker but the rest of the bus still looks great.

I have no fancy equipment.  I painted in a barn-literally.  I
also educated myself by enrolling in some auto-body courses at
the local community college before taking on the "hard" part.
The more you learn up-front, the less you have to re-do.
