Subject: Re: [T2] FI or carb

On Oct 16, 2005, at 9:28 PM, okartguy wrote:

&gt; You might have better luck with dual carbs, but keep in mind that a
&gt; '77 type IV was *designed* to run FI, and the cam and valve
&gt; configuration reflect that. Plus, dual carbs mean jacking with
&gt; synchronization, leaky throttle shafts, float problems etc. There's a
&gt; *reason* nobody makes carbed vehicles anymore!

Exactly.

It's easy to find faults with an old FI setup after installing a new  
set of carbs. My advice to those having problems with FI, is to strip  
the engine of the parts, test them individually, clean the contacts  
and rejuvenate it. Find the faulty part and replace it.

&gt; Also, if you really look at it, the AFC FI system is pretty daggummed
&gt; simple. A few sensors, the injectors and related plumbing and the
&gt; brainbox, and that's about it. The FI system on my '79 has been stone
&gt; reliable; the bus starts instantaneously in hot or cold weather, idles
&gt; like it should, runs very smoothly and is returning 20-23 mpg on 87
&gt; octane. Don't just take my word for it, but I'd look long and hard at
&gt; the pros and cons before I gave up on a gimpy FI system. Make it work!

The only downside I can see to the system is having to replace every  
part with a new one ($$$ cost) but I've never met or heard of anyone  
having to do this who had a competent mechanic.

The temp sensor II seems to fail after 100k miles of heat cycles but  
luckily it's a $12 part.

I've had to replace the cold start valve because it's spray pattern  
was poor but I considered that fix optional. I've also replaced the  
fuel pressure regulator because it couldn't hold the fuel lines  
pressure after the engine turned off. In both cases the engine ran  
well without noticeable problems but they were revealed to me during  
testing. That goes to show in part that the FI system has some fault  
tolerance engineered in.
