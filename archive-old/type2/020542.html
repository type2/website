[...]A gasless welder is by definition not a MIG welder (MIG = Metal - 
Inert Gas).  I've never tried gasless, but I've owned a 110V MIG for 
many years.  It works well for all kinds of projects, and makes very 
clean, splatter-free welds under the proper conditions (i.e., surfaces 
to be welded are clean, and the wind is not blowing away your shield 
gas).  Some of the gasless welders offer an optional gas conversion kit, 
but from what I've seen, you're better off buying a true MIG (i.e., 
equipped for gas) unit to begin with. 

I would be wary of the real cheapies.  A common problem with wire feed 
welders is "birdnesting", where the wire, which is pushed through the 
guide tube by the feed roller, bunches up due to excess friction in the 
guide tube.  That's the last thing you need when you're trying to get 
something done.  Also, pay attention to the duty cycle rating of the 
unit.  Some are only 20%, which means that, on average, you can only 
have the arc struck for 20% of the time the unit is in use.  For small 
jobs and tack welding, that's not going to be an issue, but when you're 
running a continuous long bead, it can be a major problem.  Mine is 
rated 80% at the low heat setting, and it has worked fine.
