> I learned to drive in a 1966 deluxe and have wanted to own one since my
> parents sold the bus in 1972.
> 
> There are so many memories of that bus, such as the trip from Eugene OR
> to Dunlap IA with my folks, back in 1969.  We traveled thru miles of oil
> derricks on the way... but not a gas station open for 200 miles.  When we
> ran to the "reserve", we pulled over to gas stations in hopes to find a
> few ounces of gas in the nozzle of a station (this was just after the sun
> came up, sometime around 6 AM).  The station owner had left a 5 gallon
> can of gas in case someone ran out.  We left the station happy... and
> left him a note and $10 for the gas (gas was a lot cheaper then, but we
> were very grateful)... and enough gas to drive on to other stations that
> were open.
> 
> After we reached my grandparents' house, dad decided to drive on into
> Detroit to visit a relative there.  On the way he picked up a hitchhiker
> (and, as per usual, he bought a meal for the guy when he stopped for
> breakfast).  A few hours later, the bus started losing power (dad never
> liked that bus because he didn't know how to work on it... it was a
> "thing" that mom and I had for the bus that kept it around <grin>).  He
> would drive down the hills, getting up to about 35.  On the next uphill
> stretch, they'd plod along on the shoulder until they got down to walking
> speed.  The hitchhiker would jump out and help push the bus to the top of
> the next hill.  They finally reached a large town (small city), and dad
> found the local VW expert just off the first ramp.
> 
> He stayed up at a hotel overnight, waiting results of the examination by
> the garage.  Finally, with the engine apart on the bench, they found that
> every part was damaged except the case.  They could rebuild the engine
> from parts, or could order a new engine from Detroit.  There was about
> $50 difference in price, but it'd take 3 days for the engine to arrive.
> 
> So, with 45K on the bus, it had the engine rebuilt.  Previous, the bus
> would do 70 flat out... but only 65 after the rebuild... and pedal down
> was the way the bus was always driven on the freeway.
> 
> I attribute the engine failure to 2 things.  Obviously, cylinder 3 had
> dropped a valve, and it was only a miracle that it didn't totally destroy
> the engine.  It was mid-summer and temperatures were 100+, not a good
> time to be driving the bus flat-out, and we should have had the valves
> adjusted before attempting a cross-country trek.
> 
> I'm looking for a bus of my own, and would LOVE to lay my hands on a 65
> or 66 deluxe (green).  As a concession to its "frailities", I'm
> considering on putting in a cylinder and oil temp guage... and possibly
> relocating the oil cooler (although another part of me wants a bone-stock
> VW).  BTW, while dad thought it was a 66 bus, I still believe it was a
> 65.  I bought the parts for the bus, and worked from chasis and engine
> numbers, which identified it as such.  I still think that it was a 65,
> but that he bought it in 66 (there wasn't a big difference between the
> models, but I'm sure the title also said 65).
> 
> Any way, thanks again.  There are other stories, of course... such as the
> time we took to the logging roads, with friends "hanging" out the sun
> roof, and such.  I'm looking forward to doing some traveling, and a VW
> seems such a "cool" way to get there.
> 
> As a forward, I did have a 60 beetle, complete with a true reserve tank.
>  Before I could figure out where all the gas was going (it only got
> around 10 MPG), it was wrecked by my sister (I was in the Army at the
> time).
