Being physics challenged yesterday, my point was that air with a lot
of water mixed in with it appears to be able to absorb heat better.

On thinking about it some more after I sent it, I wondered if it was 
correct....for example....

If you could instantly transport a acvw engine from the desert(0% 
humidity) to someplace like houston(100% humidity) with the air temps 
exactly the same, would it run cooler in Houston?

I suspect the answer is a resounding *no*.

What we had here was more than likely a lot of cooler water splashing up 
on the bottom of the engine and pulling more heat out that way.

What I didn't see anyone mention was the fact that a vw engine with a 
spray injector on the outside shooting into the fan would no longer be
just an air cooled engine<grin>, but a water cooled engine.

On Fri, 10 May 2002, TR Gesell wrote:

> Sorry Dave, I gotta disagree on this one too.  Humid air is actually less
> dense than dry air.  Water vapor behaves mostly like any other gas.  Dry air
> is a mixture of mostly N2 and O2.  Humid air has N2 and O2 and H20
> molecules.  H20 is about half the weight of N2 or O2.  A given volume of air
> has room for only a certain number of molecules.  If some of the N2 and O2
> are displaced by lighter H2O, then the resulting mixture is less dense.  I'm
> not sure whether dry or humid air has more capacity to absorb heat.  A
> disclaimer here:  LIQUID water will absorb lots of heat before turning into
> water vapor, so a mixture of humid air and water droplets would be a pretty
> good coolant.
