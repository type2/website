Yeah, I agree. But I think in a more broad sense, most car companies during
those times created cars to be repairable. It was just how cars were made.
People expected a car to be servicable, and people KNEW how to service cars.
It stems back to the days of the Model-T. You knew EVERYTHING about that car
when you bought it. It came with manuals to do everything.

To SOME extent, I would say the VW Bus (and other German manufacturers)
built their cars more or less to be repaired.. after all they did come with
tool kits (even if they weren't that useful). 

But, as with ALL cars, as things progressed and became more complex, fewer
and fewer people knew how to work on cars, or had the desire to bother. Say
for example, a 1969 Pontiac GTO with the MASSIVE 7.4 liter 455 Cubic Inch
V8. Most people knew how to work on those cars. They knew how to replace air
filters, spark plugs, chance the oil, etc. It was VERY easy back then.

NOW... have you ever tried to service a 2003 Volkswagen Jetta? My girlfriend
has a 1.8T that she recently bought near the end of last year. The thing is
ridiculous. You can't get to ANYTHING without having to remove some sort of
obstructionary panel.
