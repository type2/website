	Bradley, the fuel gauge IS a current meter. The movement is
a little DC motor that only turns a fraction of a full circle. Current
flowing through the coil of wire attached to the needle creates
a magnetic field that interacts with fixed magnents and moves the
needle. More current, more movement. Stop the current and the needle
returns to 0.

	The fuel tank sender is a variable resistor which provides
a variable current to the meter The fixed voltage from the little
regulator and the 'full' resistance of the sender give you a 'full'
tank indication, which IS probably a pretty low resistance. Lower
fuel, higher resitance, less current, lower meter reading. Turn it
off and there's no current, meter reads 0.

	This is exactly the same as the Bosch fuel injection
systems- constant fuel pressure and either variable size
openings in a metering body (CIS K-Jet) or varying durration of 
squirts (D-Jet) gives variable fuel volume.

	The key equation is Current = Voltage / Resistance.
In easy units of Amps = Volts / Ohms. This is Ohms law. Re-arange it
for extra credit to solve for any unknown given two knows.
