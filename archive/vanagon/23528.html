Yes, you could.

Try this, I have;  disconnect your fan belt (upright engines), attach a
variable speed drill to your alt/genny thing via a chuck attachment and the
proper socket.  Turn your ignition key on and run the alt/genny with the
drill.  With no electrical load you will feel the thing 'kick in' when the
thing reaches the rpm needed to fire the rotor.  Now start turning things
on, one by one.  Little loads (interior lights, running lights) will hardly
be felt, but when you turn on your headlights, uunnhh, the drill bogs down
significantly, i.e. ~1 hp.  That is in fact, 1 hp the engine is required to
pay to the charging system.  Make no mistake about it, electrical energy is
created with hp.  Electrical energy does not simply _appear_.  I've made
bicycle sleds that drive an alternator with a belt off the rear wheel and
attached bunches of headlights to it.  I think I could push about 4 small
headlights before I just couldn't pump the pedals hard enough, propably over
1/2 hp.  Racing cyclists are known to push 1/4 hp and 1/2 hp in sprints.
The guy who crossed the English Channel on a pedal powered aero produced 1/4
hp the whole way, with a couple peaks when he almost hit the water.
Remember when the Chinese had a law that you couldn't use your headlights at
night in order to save the countries fuel?  They finally allowed headlights
somewhere in the early seventies I think, after a few dead peasants.  Ever
wonder about the lamps inside the headlight fixtures of the splitties?  They
can be used to drive on those nights when you're on a slow road and low on
gas.  Not that anybody would get themselves in that situation, but I'm sure
it is/was common practice in the third world.  Though I'm sure the 1/2 hp
saved was eaten up easily in a few quick blips of the throttle somewhere else.
