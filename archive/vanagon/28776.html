 Anyway. I'd say replace the sealing rings. The seal is formed when this
 soft piece of metal is squeezed between two harder ones, conforming to
 the irregularities in the latter. It's kind of a "one shot" deal, unlike 
 a rubber seal which will "unconform" when unclamped. The metal seal MIGHT
 reform to a new shape, despite having been stretched and abused the first
 time, but it is so much work to get at those seals, why take a chance?



 On the topic of aluminum bodies - I thinks folks should make a clear 
 distinction between use of aluminum in stressed versus unstressed 
 applications. The Land-Rover <and other cars where a hood or a trunk is
 aluminum> uses aluminum body panels, but the vehicles structural strength
 comes from a robust, STEEL frame. Since most modern automobiles are built
 from formed sheets welded into a stress bearing structure, the fatigue
 characteristics of aluminum make it's use far more difficult. This is 
 addressed in aircraft by rigorous inspection, maintenance, and pre-emptive
 replacement. But even then, problems can occur, the Hawaiian Air 737 that
 developed a "sunroof" in mid-flight through which a flight attendant was
 sucked out as an example.

 The fatigue curve for steel mercifully flattens out so that old steel things
 can be counted on to have some high percentage of their original strength
 if corrosion hasn't taken it's toll. Aluminum, on the other hand, does not
 fatigue quickly but it does continue to do so in a pretty linear fashion,
 with strength dropping slowly over time. Failures usually appear as cracks
 in regions of highest stress.
